**VULNERABILITY**
"Exes Rate Each Other's New Partners Over Drinks… And It Gets Too Real"

*Why it works:* **PRESERVED.** This title already anchors the mechanic (exes + new partners + rating), activates the jealousy triangle for parasocial projection, and the "Too Real" escalation payoff maps to LASSO-stable escalation_intensity. No revision needed.

**CURIOSITY**
"My Ex Rated My New Partner — Then I Walked In"

*Why it works:* This is lifted directly from the revised concept's production note because it's already a perfect curiosity title. It operates on a **three-beat narrative structure**: (1) "My Ex Rated My New Partner" — establishes a scenario that's already uncomfortable, (2) "Then I Walked In" — introduces the twist reveal that the person being discussed is *present*, which is the concept's core escalation mechanic. The dash creates a pacing break that mimics a dramatic beat, boosting perceived stakes. The first-person POV ("My Ex… My New Partner… I Walked In") activates parasocial identification — the viewer *becomes* the person walking in. The information gap (What did the ex say? What happens when they walk in?) drives click-through by withholding the emotional payoff. The "walked in" language implies confrontation without promising it, threading the line between curiosity and clickbait.

---

**SHOCK**
"3 Exes Judge Each Other's New Partners… Then the New Partners Show Up"

*Why it works:* The revision brief emphasizes the **chaos energy** and the **"new partners walk in" twist** as core preserves. This title weaponizes both. "3 Exes" signals ensemble chaos (multiple dynamics, unpredictable reactions). "Judge" is harsher than "Rate" — it carries moral weight and implied cruelty that maps to the "unhinged, diabolical tone" the brief demands. The ellipsis creates a suspense beat before the reveal: "Then the New Partners Show Up." This is a **collision title** — two groups who should never be in the same room are suddenly in the same room. The shock isn't gore or outrage; it's *social dread*. Viewers click because they need to witness the moment of impact.

---

**INTRIGUE**
"We Made Exes Rate Each Other's New Partners. Nobody Was Ready."

*Why it works:* The "We Made" framing positions the creator as an **agent of chaos** — aligning with the diabolical producer tone from the brief. "Nobody Was Ready" is a universal emotional payoff that implies the reactions exceeded even the participants' expectations, which signals authentic emotional escalation (not scripted). This framing boosts credibility while maintaining intrigue. It also works as a **social proof hook** — if *nobody* was ready, the content must have hit harder than anticipated, which triggers FOMO-driven clicks. The period after "Ready" (instead of an exclamation mark) adds deadpan confidence that reads as more authentic.

---

**CHAOS**
"Exes Do Shots and Rate Each Other's New Partners — Then It Goes Off the Rails"

*Why it works:* This title leads with the **drinking game format** (a core preserve from the brief) by naming "Do Shots" explicitly, which sets tonal expectations: this is loose, unfiltered, and escalating. The drinking mechanic signals that inhibitions will drop, priming the viewer for raw honesty. "Goes Off the Rails" is a chaos-coded payoff that implies the format itself breaks down — participants stop playing the game and start saying things they shouldn't. This maps to the brief's emphasis on the format creating conditions where "brutally honest questions" spiral into genuine emotional confrontation. The dash creates a before/after structure: controlled game → uncontrolled reality.
