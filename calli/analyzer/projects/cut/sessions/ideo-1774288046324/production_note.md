PRODUCTION NOTE: "EXES PLAY TRUTH OR DRINK — BUT THEY'RE STILL IN LOVE"
CUT Analytics · Format Development · Senior Producer Brief

═══ PART A: PRODUCTION NOTE ═══

Concept
Two exes who everyone around them knows are still in love sit across from each other for a game of Truth or Drink — but neither has admitted it since the breakup, and the questions escalate from playful nostalgia into territory that forces one of them to either confess or drink their way through the most obvious feelings in the room. This isn't the standard "exes rehash drama" play. The core tension is the gap between what the audience can see on their faces and what they're willing to say out loud. We're weaponizing the Truth or Drink mechanic not for shock, but for emotional exposure — the drink becomes a visible act of cowardice, and the truth becomes an act of courage the audience is begging for.
The Hook: Two people who broke up eight months ago sit down, and within the first fifteen seconds, the way she looks at him when he's not watching tells you everything. The camera catches the micro-expression before a single word is spoken. The curiosity gap is immediate: you already know the answer — but will THEY say it?
The WTF Moment: Round 5. The question is: "What's the real reason you ended it?" Marcus reaches for the shot. Jasmine says, quietly, "Don't." He puts the glass down. She says: "Because I was scared that if I stayed, I'd never want anything else." The table goes silent. Marcus exhales and says, "That's the worst reason I've ever heard." And then he's crying. That's the clip. That's 40 million impressions on TikTok.

Thumbnail & Title Strategy
The thumbnail is the product promise. It must sell the two-person dynamic — not a solo reaction. The frame is a mid-game moment pulled from Round 5: Marcus and Jasmine both visible in a tight two-shot, shot across the table at eye level. She's mid-sentence, mouth slightly open, eyes wet. He's frozen — jaw tight, looking directly at her, one hand still hovering near the shot glass he just put down. The body language is unmistakable: something just detonated between these two people. This is not posed. This is pulled from footage. The energy is caught, not constructed.

Title overlay: **"SHE ALMOST SAID IT 👀"** — white bold text, slightly offset to the lower third so it doesn't obscure either face. The "almost" does the work. It tells the viewer: there's a confession in this video that nearly happened, and you need to find out if it lands. The eyes emoji is the voyeuristic permission slip — *you're allowed to watch this.* The pronoun "she" genders the tension just enough to activate romantic curiosity without spoiling the arc. Combined with two faces in visible emotional distress, the thumbnail reads as: *unresolved love, caught live, one of them is about to break.* That's the click.

Why this reframe matters: A solo reaction shot reads as commentary content — someone reacting to something off-screen. It buries the relationship, which is the entire product. Two faces in one frame immediately communicates *tension between two specific people*. The viewer's brain processes the dynamic before they read a single word. The title then names what the dynamic is: an almost-confession. Frame + title together create a curiosity gap that can only be closed by watching. One frame. Two faces. One unfinished sentence.

The Loop
Core Loop: Question asked → participant must choose truth or drink → truth reveals buried feeling (or drink reveals fear of it) → the other person reacts → emotional stakes compound for the next round.
Loop Family: Truth/Reveal + Pressure
Why This Loop Fits: Truth or Drink is the purest reveal loop in our library — every round is a binary choice with visible consequences. The drink is the data. When someone drinks, the audience learns as much as when they speak, because the avoidance tells the story. The visual escalation of drinking reinforces the loop's stakes.
Secondary Loops:

Nostalgia trigger → emotional softening → guard drops → vulnerability spike
Deflection attempt → best friend callout → forced re-engagement → deeper honesty

Currency of tension: Emotional exposure. Every truth costs dignity; every drink costs credibility. By round 4, drinking looks pathetic and truth looks heroic. The audience is keeping a mental scorecard of who's been braver.
Ramp: Hybrid staircase-tightrope. Rounds 1-3 are a staircase (playful → personal → pointed). Rounds 4-6 are a tightrope — any answer could end the game or change everything. The compression matters.
Payoff: Will they admit they're still in love — and if they do, what happens next?

Psychological Drivers
Primary Drivers:

Vulnerability as spectacle: The audience is watching two people do the thing they're most afraid of — being honest about love — in real time. Every second of silence before an answer is electric.
Prediction tension: Viewers are constantly forecasting: will they drink or tell the truth? The binary choice creates a natural prediction engine that fires every 45-60 seconds.
Rejection anxiety (vicarious): The terror of saying "I still love you" and hearing nothing back. The audience feels this in their chest.
Recognition / "That's me" identification: Every person watching has been in a situation where they didn't say the thing. This format is a mirror.

Secondary Drivers:

Second-hand embarrassment: When Jasmine deflects with a joke and Marcus's face falls — the cringe is productive, it makes the audience want the resolution more.
Romantic hope: Gen Z is deeply romantic under the irony. They want these two to work out. The format exploits that hope.
Social comparison: "Would I have the courage to say that?" Every round is an implicit self-assessment.
Authenticity hunger: In a feed full of performance, watching someone's voice crack while telling the truth is content gold.
Closure fantasy: Many viewers never got closure from their own relationships. This format offers it vicariously.

Emotional Design
Tone: Warm, intimate, aching. This is not a party game. This is two people sitting in the wreckage of something beautiful and deciding whether to be honest about it. The humor comes from the discomfort, not from bits.
Dominant Emotions: Longing, tenderness, tension, hope, catharsis.
Emotional Arc: Guarded warmth → playful tension → visible discomfort → emotional breakthrough → bittersweet resolution.
Tension Source: The gap between what's obvious and what's spoken. The audience can see the love. The question is whether the participants will name it.
Humor: Drea's reactions, Lo's dry delivery, and the absurdity of two people clearly in love pretending they're fine. The levity must earn the intensity.
Peak Moments:

1:45 — Recognition. Marcus's sincere Round 1 answer vs. Jasmine's joke. The audience picks a side.
3:30 — Tension spike. Marcus drinks for the first time. Jasmine's reaction — she didn't expect it to hurt watching him avoid a question.
5:15 — The WTF Moment. "Because I was scared..." Full emotional detonation.
6:30 — Hope. Round 6 answer. Both say yes. Neither can look at each other.
8:00 — Catharsis. The letter. Marcus's face. Audience in ruins.


Production Design
Set: Minimal. A wooden table, two chairs, warm overhead pendant light, dark background — the set should feel like 2 AM at a bar that's about to close. Set mood is a retention variable.
Camera: Three cameras — two tight singles (50mm equivalent, shallow DOF) and one wide establishing. The singles are the workhorses. We live on faces. NOTE FOR THUMBNAIL CAPTURE: The wide camera must be positioned to frame both faces in a single compelling image — slightly off-center, across the table, catching the diagonal of their eye contact or near-eye contact. During Round 5, the wide camera operator flags the "Don't" moment for thumbnail pull. We need both faces in one frame at peak emotional load. This is not optional — the thumbnail is the front door.
Graphics: Lower thirds for names/relationship context only. Round number appears briefly. No score overlays — this isn't a game show, it's an emotional excavation. Title overlay for thumbnail/social: **"SHE ALMOST SAID IT 👀"** — tested against "ALMOST SAID IT" and "THEY'RE STILL IN LOVE AND EVERYONE KNOWS." The pronoun version wins on curiosity and specificity.
Music & Sound: Sparse piano and ambient pads underneath, pulled almost entirely during key answers. The sound of the shot glass hitting the table should be audible and satisfying. No music during the WTF moment — silence is the score.
Testimonials: Post-game confessionals shot immediately after, same setup, solo. Each person gets 90 seconds. These are B-roll gold and potential standalone shorts.
