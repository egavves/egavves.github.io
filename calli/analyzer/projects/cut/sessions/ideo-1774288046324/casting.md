## Demographics & Energy

**Age Range:** 20–27 (core), with one possible outlier at 19 or 28 to create a subtle generational friction point.

**Personality Types:** We need a mix of high-verbal processors (people who think out loud, stumble into honesty) and slow-burn reactors (people whose silence *is* the content). No one should feel "produced." Everyone should feel like someone you'd overhear at a house party saying something that makes you put your drink down.

**Diversity:** Non-negotiable across race, ethnicity, gender identity, sexuality, body type, socioeconomic background, and regional upbringing. But diversity here isn't a checklist — it's a *chemistry ingredient*. We're casting for the moments where someone says "Wait, that's not how it works in YOUR family?" and an entire worldview cracks open on camera. [[H01: Diverse lived-experience pairings generate 2.3x more "replay moments" than demographically homogeneous groups|diversity as chemistry catalyst]].

---

## Character Archetypes Needed

We don't cast "characters." We cast *energies* that emerge under pressure. Here's what the table needs:

| Archetype | What It Looks Like in This Format | Casting Note |
|---|---|---|
| **The Hero** | Goes first on the hardest question. Doesn't flinch. Makes vulnerability look easy, which gives everyone else permission. | Often misidentified as "the confident one" — actually the most emotionally regulated person in the room. |
| **The Villain** | Asks the follow-up question the producers didn't write. Pushes someone past their rehearsed answer. Grins while doing it. | NOT mean. Charismatic disruptor. The audience should be mad at them and also texting their friend "you'd love this person." |
| **The Underdog** | Starts quiet. Seems like they might tap out. Then drops the most devastating truth of the entire episode around minute six. | Cast someone who self-describes as "bad at this kind of thing." They're lying. They're the best at it. |
| **The Overconfident** | Swears they'll never drink. Drinks by question three. Their arc IS the episode's comedic spine. | Look for someone whose bravado is clearly a love language, not a wall. |
| **The Wildcard** | Unpredictable response cadence. Might answer a light question with something devastating. Might answer a heavy question with a joke that somehow lands harder. | This person breaks the rhythm every time the audience thinks they've figured out the format. [[H02: Wildcard archetype presence correlates with 40%+ increase in comment section engagement and "I need a Part 2" requests|wildcard as retention engine]]. |
| **The Super Fun** | Pure joy energy. Laughs before the question is finished. Makes everyone else laugh. Keeps the room from collapsing when it gets too heavy. | Critical role. Without this person, vulnerability becomes suffering. With them, vulnerability becomes *connection*. |

Ideal table size: **5–6 people.** Enough for cross-talk, small enough that everyone gets a real arc.

---

## What We Value (In Order)

1. **Comfort on camera under pressure** — Can they sit in an awkward silence without performing discomfort? Can they hold eye contact with someone who just said something real? We're not looking for people who are good on camera. We're looking for people who *forget* the camera.

2. **Experience with vulnerability formats** — Podcasts, support groups, group therapy, slam poetry, even just being "the friend everyone calls at 2 AM." We want people whose emotional reflexes are trained, not rehearsed.

3. **Social native fluency** — They understand the language, pacing, and unspoken rules of short-form content *as viewers*, not necessarily as creators. They know what a "moment" feels like because they've screenshot-shared hundreds of them.

4. **Group chemistry instinct** — Can they read a room mid-sentence and adjust? Do they know when to push and when to hold space? This is the hardest thing to screen for and the most important thing on shoot day.

---

## Red Flags to Avoid

| Red Flag | Why |
|---|---|
| **Follower count as a primary qualifier** | Vanity metric. A person with 800 followers who makes their group chat cry-laugh weekly is worth more than someone with 200K who hasn't said anything real since 2021. |
| **Traditional acting credits** | We need people who *react*. Trained actors often *respond* — there's a difference. The micro-expressions we're after can't be manufactured. |
| **Brand deal history / influencer positioning** | If someone's instinct is to protect their "brand," they will pull punches at exactly the moments we need them to swing. [[H03: Talent with active brand partnerships show 35% higher likelihood of deflecting high-vulnerability questions with humor shields vs. genuine engagement|brand-safety instinct as vulnerability inhibitor]]. |
| **"I'm so random" self-description** | Manufactured unpredictability is the opposite of what we need. |
| **Anyone who asks about final cut approval** | This is a trust format. If the trust doesn't start in pre-production, it won't exist on camera. |

---

## Gender Mix & Regional Voice

**Gender Mix:** No fixed ratio. The table should feel like a real friend group, not a panel. Aim for representation across the gender spectrum — cis men, cis women, nonbinary, trans, and gender-fluid talent — but never tokenize. If someone is the "only ___" at the table, ask whether the casting is serving them or using them.

**Regional Voice:** This matters enormously and is chronically under-cast. We want:
- A Southern voice that isn't a punchline
- A Midwest earnestness that catches people off guard
- A coastal sharpness (NYC, LA, Bay Area) that can be punctured
- At least one person whose first language isn't English, whose bilingual code-switching IS their personality
- Rural and suburban backgrounds alongside urban ones

Regional diversity is *tonal* diversity. It changes the rhythm of the conversation, the idioms, the comfort thresholds, the humor. A table full of people who all sound like Brooklyn is a table that will never surprise us.

---

## Summary Casting North Star

We are not casting for entertainment. We are casting for **the moment someone almost doesn't say it — and then does.** Every person at this table should be someone the audience wants to *know*, not just watch. The drink is a prop. The truth is the product. The people are the reason anyone cares.

## SCENE 2 — "THE WARM-UP" (ROUND 1: LIGHT PRESSURE)

**[1:00–3:30]**

*[Questions appear on screen as physical cards flipped over on the table. The person who flips reads aloud.]*

**CARD 1** *(flipped by SAM, 23, rural Oregon, quiet energy):*
"What's a lie you tell people about yourself that you've told so many times it basically feels true?"

*[SAM reads it, then immediately looks down.]*

**SAM:**
"...I'll go first, I guess."

*(pause)*

"I tell people I moved to Portland for a job. I moved to Portland because I couldn't be myself in my hometown and I was scared I was going to disappear."

*[Table goes silent. This was supposed to be the warm-up round.]*

**JESS** *(softly):*
"Sam."

**SAM** *(small laugh, waving it off):*
"No, no, it's fine, I'm good now. I have a cat and a therapist. I'm thriving."

*[Laughter. Relief. But the room has shifted. Everyone knows what kind of episode this is now.]*

**CARD 2** *(flipped by KAI, 20, Bay Area, Wildcard energy):*
"What's something you judge other people for that you also do?"

**KAI:**
"Oh, I'll answer this. I judge people who post Instagram stories at concerts. And then I post Instagram stories at concerts. Every single time. I'm not even conflicted about it."

**MARCUS:**
"That's not a confession, that's just being a person."

**KAI:**
"Okay fine. I judge people who stay in bad relationships. And I have literally never left a relationship. They've all left me. Because I would've stayed forever. Every single one."

*[Beat.]*

**DEZ:**
"...Kai."

**KAI** *(shrugs, smiling):*
"You wanted truth."
