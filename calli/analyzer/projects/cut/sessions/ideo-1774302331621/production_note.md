PRODUCTION NOTE: "EXES PLAY TRUTH OR DRINK — BUT THEIR NEW PARTNERS ARE WATCHING"

═══ PART A: PRODUCTION NOTE ═══

Concept
Two ex-couples sit across from each other at a table loaded with shots, answering increasingly invasive questions about their past relationship — but their current partners are seated behind a one-way mirror, watching and reacting in real time, with the power to send in ONE question each at any point during the game. The core tension isn't between the exes; it's between what someone is willing to reveal about their past when the person they're building a future with is silently judging every answer.

But the current partners aren't just watching. Each one has a single-use intervention button — a red button mounted on the counter in front of them that, when pressed, kills the lights at the table, opens the mirror room door, and lets them walk in and confront their partner face-to-face, mid-game. They can use it once. They can use it never. But the exes at the table don't know the button exists. They think they're being watched. They don't know they can be interrupted.

The Hook: "Your new boyfriend is watching everything you say right now. He also has a button that lets him walk in at any moment. You don't know about the button. He does." That single asymmetry transforms every answer from a gamble into a potential detonation. The viewer understands three things instantly: honesty with the ex means risk with the current partner, every drink dodged is its own confession, and at any moment, the mirror could open. The question isn't just "will they be honest?" — it's "will this answer be the one that brings someone through that door?"

The WTF Moment: At 5:42, Priya is asked "Did you ever fake it with your ex?" She pauses, glances at the mirror, and says "Which time?" — and the cut to her current boyfriend Deshawn behind the glass, jaw literally dropping, hands on his head, his finger hovering six inches above the red button before he pulls it back, is the single most clippable five seconds we'll produce this quarter. Priya's "Which time?" moment is a clip machine. The almost-press is the sequel clip.

Cast
Priya (26) — The Disarmingly Honest One. Former psychology student who treats emotional conversations like academic exercises — she'll dissect her own heartbreak with clinical precision and a half-smile. She broke up with Marcus two years ago because she "outgrew the version of herself that needed him," which she will say to his face. Reveal: "I wrote you a letter after we broke up. I never sent it. I still have it on my phone."
Archetype: Hero

Marcus (28) — The Still-Carrying-It One. Works in construction, communicates in short sentences, and has clearly not processed the breakup. He's the guy who says "I'm good" when he's absolutely not good. His vulnerability comes not from eloquence but from the visible effort it takes him to say anything real. Reveal: "I drove past your apartment for three months after. I don't know why."
Archetype: Underdog

Jalen (24) — The Chaotic Charmer. Dated Sofia for eight months and will tell you it was "the most fun and the most toxic eight months of my life" while grinning. He's the energy in the room — loud, flirtatious, incapable of reading the temperature. He'll say something devastating and then laugh. Reveal: "I told my boys I broke up with you. You broke up with me. I just couldn't say it."
Archetype: Chaotic Wildcard / Super Fun

Sofia (25) — The One Who Won't Flinch. Competitive, sharp, treats the game like a game. She'll answer anything, drink nothing, and stare Marcus down while doing it. She's not here to reconnect — she's here to win. But her armor cracks exactly once. Reveal: "I'm scared my new boyfriend is going to see who I was with you and not want who I am now."
Archetype: Villain (surface) / Heartwarming (ending)

Deshawn (27) — The Current Partner (Behind Glass). Priya's boyfriend. Confident, secure, but you can see the micro-expressions shift as revelations land. He's the audience surrogate — reacting the way we react. His hand drifts toward the button three times before he finally decides what to do with it. His single sent-in question will be devastating in its simplicity — but the real question the audience is tracking is: will he press the button instead?
Archetype: Gatekeeper / Audience Surrogate

Mia (23) — The Current Partner (Behind Glass). Jalen's girlfriend. Quieter, more anxious, fidgeting with her rings. She represents the fear every viewer has: what if I find out something I can't un-know? She presses the button early — too early — and the consequences of that impulsive choice reshape the entire back half of the episode.
Archetype: Underdog (secondary)

Chemistry & Group Dynamic: The table energy oscillates between Jalen's reckless charisma and Marcus's heavy silence. Priya and Sofia are both sharp but in different registers — Priya is warm-sharp, Sofia is cold-sharp. The invisible presence of Deshawn and Mia creates a phantom tension: every laugh between exes feels like a betrayal, every honest answer feels like a gift and a wound simultaneously. The button adds a third frequency — the possibility of physical intrusion — that hums beneath every exchange like a bass note. The group doesn't need a host because the format is the host — the questions, the mirror, and the button do all the work.

The Loop
Core Loop: Question asked → ex must answer or drink → answer lands → cut to current partner's reaction behind glass (and their proximity to the button) → consequence ripples forward into next question

Loop Family: Truth/Reveal + Gatekeeper (the current partners function as invisible gatekeepers whose eventual question AND potential intervention raise stakes)

Why This Loop Fits: Truth or Drink already has a clean binary loop, but adding the surveillance layer doubles every payoff — each answer resolves one tension (will they be honest?) while creating another (how does the current partner feel?). The deferred "partner question" acts as a ticking clock that escalates without any dialogue. The intervention button adds a third tension layer: every cut behind the glass now carries the threat of physical consequence. The audience isn't just watching reactions — they're watching a decision being made in real time. Will this be the answer that brings someone through the door?

Secondary Loops:

Partner reaction + button proximity → viewer prediction ("is this a dealbreaker? will they press it?") → confirmation/surprise
Drink refusal → implied confession → partner interpretation → hand moves toward button → tension compounds
Partner question card → delivery → ex's response → aftermath
Button press → lights kill → door opens → confrontation → table dynamic permanently altered

Currency of tension: Relational capital. Every answer spends trust with one person to buy honesty with another. The drinks aren't punishment — they're evidence of what you won't say, which is sometimes worse. The button isn't punishment either — it's the moment someone decides they've heard enough and needs to be in the room. The threat of it is worth more than the press.

Ramp: Hybrid staircase/tightrope. Questions escalate in three tiers (nostalgia → intimacy → unresolved feelings), but the partner questions and the button can arrive at any moment, creating tightrope unpredictability within a staircase structure. The button is a format-level wild card — it can collapse the staircase into freefall at any point.

Payoff: "Will the current partners still want to be with them after hearing everything — and will they wait to find out, or break through the glass to stop it?"

Psychological Drivers
Primary Drivers:

Vulnerability under surveillance: Every participant knows they're being watched by the person whose opinion matters most, which transforms routine honesty into high-wire emotional performance.
Prediction tension (dual-track): Viewers are constantly predicting — will they answer or drink? Will the partner react with anger or understanding? Will this be the answer that triggers the button? Three prediction engines running simultaneously.
Rejection anxiety (vicarious): The fear that honesty about a past relationship will destroy a current one is universally relatable and viscerally uncomfortable.
Jealousy activation: Watching your partner laugh with or confess feelings about an ex triggers primal jealousy responses that viewers experience secondhand.
Intervention anticipation: The button creates a Hitchcock bomb — the audience knows it's there, the exes don't, and every second it isn't pressed increases the tension. Viewers are simultaneously dreading and craving the moment someone uses it.

Secondary Drivers:

Second-hand embarrassment: Jalen's inability to read the room and Marcus's visible discomfort create cringe-empathy that keeps viewers locked in.
Self-projection: Every viewer with an ex and a current partner is mentally answering these questions themselves, calculating their own risk tolerance — and asking themselves, "Would I press the button?"
Authenticity detection: The drink mechanic is a lie detector — viewers are scanning for micro-expressions, hesitations, and strategic drinks that reveal more than answers would.
Moral judgment: The format invites viewers to judge who's "winning" the breakup, who's more evolved, and who's still lying to themselves.
Protective instinct: Viewers develop protective feelings toward Deshawn and Mia behind the glass, wanting to warn them or comfort them — and opinions split on whether pressing the button is strength or weakness.

Story Structure
Act 1: The Setup (0:00–2:00) — Establish the arena and the stakes

Cold open: Priya's "Which time?" moment (flash-forward tease) — intercut with Deshawn's hand hovering over the button
Title card / format explanation (10 seconds max)
Exes sit down, awkward greetings, first glimpse of the one-way mirror
Current partners revealed behind glass — their faces when they realize what's happening. Then the producer explains the button. Deshawn: "So I can just... walk in there?" Producer: "Once. Whenever you want. She doesn't know you can." Deshawn stares at the button. Mia immediately puts her hand on hers, then pulls it back. The audience now understands the stakes are physical, not just emotional.
First question (icebreaker): nervous laughter, everyone's feeling each other out

Act 2: The Escalation (2:00–5:30) — Questions get personal, drinks start flowing

Transition from "when did you know it was over?" to "what's something you never told them?"
Marcus's first real answer breaks the ice — the room shifts
Jalen answers everything, drinks nothing, which starts to worry Mia behind the glass — her hand keeps drifting to the button, pulling back, drifting again
Sofia drinks for the first time — the table goes silent
Partner reaction intercuts intensify — every cut behind the glass now includes the button in frame, a constant visual reminder of the loaded gun on the counter
At 4:45, Mia presses the button. The lights at the table cut. Everyone freezes. The mirror door clicks open. Mia walks in. Jalen's face — the moment he realizes what's been happening, that she's been watching, that she heard everything — is the second-biggest moment in the episode. She doesn't yell. She just says: "Did you mean what you said about her?" The table is silent. Jalen, for the first time, has nothing to say. Mia sits down in an empty chair. She's now at the table. The game continues with five people and one button still unused.

Act 3: The Detonation (5:30–8:00) — Partner questions arrive, walls come down

Mia's presence at the table changes everything — Jalen can't perform anymore, every answer is accountable in real time
Deshawn's question card is delivered to Priya: "Is there anything about him you miss that I don't give you?"
Priya's answer — honest, careful, devastating
She looks at the mirror after answering. She's talking to Deshawn now, not Marcus: "I know you're in there. I'd say all of this to your face."
Deshawn's hand is on the button. He doesn't press it. The restraint is its own answer — and the audience splits on whether it's trust or paralysis.
Sofia's armor-crack moment — her reveal about fear — hits harder now because Mia's intervention proved the fear is real: the person behind the glass can come through

Act 4: The Aftermath (8:00–9:30) — Reunions and resolution

Deshawn comes out from behind the glass — through the door, not the button. His choice to walk out calmly instead of intervening says everything.
Deshawn and Priya's conversation — he's quiet, then he hugs her. "I didn't press it because I didn't need to. I trust you." Priya cries for the first time.
Mia and Jalen — she's crying, he's apologizing, it's messy and real. Her pressing the button early was impulsive and brave and possibly relationship-ending and the audience won't agree on whether it was the right call. That's the point.
Marcus, alone at the table, picks up the last shot and drinks it. Smiles. End card.

This minute is the investment lock — viewers now need to know what Marcus is hiding, whether Jalen's charm is real, and who presses the button first.

Emotional Design
Tone: Warm but unflinching. The lighting says "safe space," the questions say "nowhere to hide," the button says "and there are consequences." Think therapy session energy with party game mechanics and a panic button.

Dominant Emotions: Tension, tenderness, cringe, catharsis, jealousy, relief, dread.

Emotional Arc: Nervous laughter → growing discomfort → raw honesty → intervention (Mia's button press is the emotional earthquake at the midpoint) → recalibrated honesty with higher stakes → emotional detonation → bittersweet resolution. The arc bends toward hope — these people are brave, and the format rewards their courage. But Mia's early press proves the consequences are real, which makes Deshawn's restraint in the back half exponentially more powerful.

Tension Source: The dual-audience problem, now weaponized. You can't be fully honest with your ex without risking your current relationship — and your current partner can physically enter the conversation at any moment to make you answer for it. The button transforms passive surveillance into active threat.

Humor: Jalen provides 80% of the levity. His recklessness is funny until Mia walks through the door. The levity-to-intensity balance is critical — too much humor and the emotional moments feel unearned.

Peak Moments:

1:45 — "He still wears that cologne." Nostalgia. Priya's offhand comment lands like a depth charge. Deshawn's face. His eyes flick to the button.
3:20 — Marcus drinks instead of answering. Tension. The loudest silence in the episode. Everyone at the table knows what the drink means.
4:45 — Mia presses the button. The lights cut. Shock. The format's promise made real. The table doesn't know what's happening for two seconds, and those two seconds are electric.
5:42 — "Which time?" Shock/humor. Priya's most unfiltered moment. The clip. Deshawn's hand hovers over the button. He pulls back.
6:30 — Deshawn's question arrives. Dread. The envelope. The reading. The pause before Priya answers. The audience is now watching two things: her answer and whether he finally presses it.
8:45 — Deshawn walks out through the door — not the button. Catharsis. He heard everything. He chose to stay. He chose to come out on his own terms. The audience exhales.

Production Design
Set: Minimalist table for four (with one empty chair that becomes significant when Mia sits down), warm wood tones, soft overhead pendant light, visible one-way mirror on the back wall — the mirror should be architecturally prominent, a constant visual reminder of surveillance. Behind the glass: two separate viewing stations, each with a monitor, a question card slot, and a red button mounted on the counter, lit from below with a soft glow. The button should be beautiful and ominous — the kind of object your hand wants to touch. Camera setup behind the glass should capture both the partner's face and the button in the same frame at all times, so every reaction shot carries the implicit question: will they press it?
