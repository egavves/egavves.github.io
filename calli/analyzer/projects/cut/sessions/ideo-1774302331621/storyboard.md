# CUT Analytics — Storyboard v2.1

**"EXES: Truth or Drink"**

**Format:** Two exes sit across from each other. Their current partners watch from behind a one-way mirror and react in real-time (split-screen cutaways). Each watching partner holds ONE intervention card — a single question they can send in at any moment during the game.

**Duration Target:** 5–7 min (edited), ~45 min raw shoot

**Concept Commitment:** EXES + WATCHING PARTNERS BEHIND ONE-WAY MIRROR — the core tension is between what gets said at the table and what the current partners are absorbing in silence. The asymmetric power dynamic (they can see everything; the exes can't see them) is the engine. The single intervention question per partner is the ticking clock.

---

## SCENE 1 — "The Sit-Down"

**Visual Description:**
Cold open, no intro card. Camera starts tight on two empty chairs at a small round table with a bottle of tequila and two shot glasses, then *racks focus past the table* to reveal the one-way mirror behind — a dark, reflective surface that looks like a simple design wall. The audience knows what it is; the exes don't feel it yet. Then DEV (25) and MAYA (23) enter from opposite sides of frame. Lighting is CUT's signature flat, warm key light against a seamless cream backdrop — but we add a subtle cool-tone edge light to each subject to create a slight visual tension. Two-camera setup: Camera A on a wide two-shot (mirror visible in background, a constant silent presence), Camera B punching in for singles. The mood is electric awkwardness — forced smiles, nervous laughter before anyone speaks.

**On-Screen Action & Key Dialogue:**
Dev and Maya sit down, make eye contact, and immediately break into uncomfortable laughter. A title card smashes on screen: **"They dated for 2 years. Their current partners are watching."** A beat — then a second line fades in beneath it: **"They each get to send in one question. He doesn't know when."** Dev says, "This is already the worst idea I've ever agreed to." Maya replies, "Second worst. The first was dating you." — first laugh beat lands. Neither of them glances at the mirror. The audience does.

**Mood:** 😬 Tense anticipation with nervous humor. A layer of dramatic irony — we know what's behind the glass.

---

## SCENE 2 — "The Warm-Up Round"

**Visual Description:**
Camera B shifts to medium close-ups, alternating with each question. We introduce the MIRROR ROOM for the first time via a hard cut — not picture-in-picture, but a full-frame reveal: current partners ALEX and SAM sitting on a couch in a dim room, faces lit almost entirely by the glow of the one-way mirror in front of them, through which we can see Dev and Maya's backs at the table. The mirror room is lit with cooler, bluer tones — visually marking it as a separate emotional space, a surveillance booth. Alex has a folded card in hand. Sam is already leaning forward. After this establishing shot, we shift to a **split-screen format**: main table on the left (warm tones), mirror room reactions on the right (cool tones), with the division creating an implicit voyeuristic tension. The split-screen is used *selectively* — it appears only when a reaction in the mirror room is worth showing, then drops away, keeping the audience aware that the partners are always there even when we can't see them.

**On-Screen Action & Key Dialogue:**
Question cards appear as bold, oversized text that briefly fills the full frame before cutting to the players. **Round 1 questions are soft lobs** — "What's one thing you actually miss about the relationship?" / "Who said 'I love you' first?" Dev answers honestly, casually. Maya matches his energy. The answers are warm, even sweet — but every time something tender is said, we CUT TO the mirror room: Alex's jaw tightens almost imperceptibly. Sam writes something on her intervention card, then crosses it out. The subtext is loud: *the exes are comfortable with each other, and the current partners are registering that comfort as a threat.* A subtle graphic appears in the corner of the mirror room frame — two card icons, both still intact. The clock is ticking, but no one has played their card yet.

**Mood:** 🫠 Deceptively light — warmth at the table, quiet tension behind the glass. The audience starts tracking two emotional frequencies simultaneously.

---

## SCENE 3 — "The Escalation"

**Visual Description:**
The lighting on the main set subtly shifts — the warm key light remains, but the cool edge lights intensify slightly, as if the room itself is registering the rising stakes. Camera A tightens the wide shot so the one-way mirror now occupies more of the background frame — it's no longer set dressing, it's *looming*. Camera B begins using slower, more deliberate push-ins on faces during answers, letting silence and micro-expressions do the work. In the mirror room, we go handheld for the first time — the camera drifts between Alex and Sam, catching unguarded reactions, a hand gripping a knee, a whispered "what the fuck" that's barely audible.

**On-Screen Action & Key Dialogue:**
**Round 2 questions hit harder.** "Did you ever cheat — emotionally or physically?" / "What's one thing your current partner doesn't do that your ex did better?" Dev hesitates for the first time. He reaches for a shot glass — then pulls his hand back and answers instead: "Maya was better at... she actually fought with me. Like, she cared enough to yell." The split-screen SNAPS on: Alex in the mirror room, completely still. Then Maya, emboldened: "You were the most honest person I ever dated. That's not always a compliment." Dev laughs, but it's hollow. A question card appears: "Who ended it, and do you think they were right to?" Long pause. Maya takes a shot instead of answering. Dev watches her drink and says quietly, "That answers it." **This is the moment the audience realizes the exes still have unresolved weight between them — and that the people behind the glass can feel it.**

**Mood:** 🔥📉 Escalation. The table gets heavier. The mirror room gets quieter. The gap between what's said and what's felt starts widening.

---

## SCENE 4 — "The Intervention"

**Visual Description:**
A production assistant's hand slides a folded card under the edge of the one-way mirror through a slim pass-through slot — it appears on the main table as if from nowhere. Camera B catches Dev's face the instant he sees it: confusion, then recognition, then something close to dread. Camera A holds wide to show both exes looking at the card, then at the mirror — for the first time, *really looking at it*. The mirror room camera captures the moment from behind the glass: we see Dev and Maya's faces through it while Alex and Sam are reflected faintly in the foreground — a layered, almost ghostly composition. **This is the visual climax of the surveillance mechanic.** The split-screen is OFF. We go full-frame, cutting between rooms with hard, rhythmic edits.

**On-Screen Action & Key Dialogue:**
Dev picks up the card. Reads it silently. His expression shifts. He reads it aloud: **"Do you think you'd still be together if the timing had been different?"** (This is Alex's card — his current partner's one question.) The room goes dead quiet. Maya looks at the mirror. "They're in there right now?" Dev nods. "Yeah." Beat. Maya: "Do you want to take a shot instead?" Dev, looking directly at the mirror now — knowing Alex is behind it, unable to see through but *feeling* the gaze: "No. I think she deserves an honest answer." He pauses. "I think if we'd met two years later... maybe. But I wouldn't have met Alex two years later. So no. I don't wish the timing was different." **The mirror room: Alex exhales. It's not relief — it's something more complicated.** Sam still has her card. She's holding it tighter now.

**Mood:** 💀🫁 The air leaves the room. This is the scene the episode is built around. Intimacy as spectator sport — and the moment the fourth wall between the two rooms becomes emotionally transparent.

---

## SCENE 5 — "The Second Card"

**Visual Description:**
We stay in the aftermath of Scene 4 — the energy has shifted from performance to something raw. Camera B is now almost uncomfortably close on faces, shallow depth of field, catching wet eyes and swallowed words. The main set lighting has settled into a more neutral tone — the warm/cool tension has resolved into something honest and even. In the mirror room, Sam stands up. She's been holding her card the entire game. The handheld camera follows her as she walks to the pass-through slot. We hear the *scrape* of the card sliding through before we see it arrive on the table.

**On-Screen Action & Key Dialogue:**
Maya picks up the card. Reads it. Closes her eyes for a second. Reads it aloud: **"Did you ever talk about him the way you talk about him right now — when you were with me?"** (Sam's card — pointed, vulnerable, devastating.) Maya's voice cracks: "Sam..." — she says it to the mirror, not to Dev. Then, to the room: "No. I didn't. And that's not because I didn't feel it — it's because I didn't have the language for it yet. You taught me that language." She's talking to Sam through the glass. Dev is quiet. He takes a shot — not because he won't answer, but because this isn't his moment. **The mirror room: Sam is crying. Not dramatically. Just — tears, and a nod. She mouths "okay."**

**Mood:** 😭🪞 Emotional peak. The mirror becomes a membrane — separation and connection at once. The format delivers its thesis: *the most dangerous thing isn't what your ex knows about you; it's what your current partner learns about themselves by watching.*

---

## SCENE 6 — "The Cooldown"

**Visual Description:**
Camera A pulls back to the original wide two-shot from Scene 1 — a visual bookend. The one-way mirror is visible again in the background, but now it reads differently: not as a surveillance tool, but as a threshold. The lighting returns to full warm. Camera B captures one final detail shot: the tequila bottle, noticeably lower. The shot glasses. The crumpled question cards. The two intervention cards, now open and face-up on the table like spent ammunition.

**On-Screen Action & Key Dialogue:**
Dev and Maya sit in comfortable silence for a beat. Dev: "You think they're okay in there?" Maya: "I think they know us better now than they did an hour ago. Whether that's 'okay'..." she trails off, half-smiles. Dev raises his shot glass — still has one left. Maya picks hers up. They clink. Drink. No toast, no punchline. **Final title card: "Alex and Sam joined them at the table after filming. They talked for another two hours. The cameras were off."** Hard cut to black. CUT Analytics logo. No end card interview — the story is complete.

**Mood:** 🫂 Quiet resolution. Not tied in a bow — but landed. The format's emotional architecture holds: tension → escalation → rupture → repair (tentative). The mirror is the metaphor. The cards are the catalyst. The silence is the content.

**POST-PRODUCTION NOTES:**

- **Music:** Minimal. Ambient tone under Scenes 1–2, pulling out entirely by Scene 4. No music during card reads. A single, spare piano motif under Scene 6's final beat.
- **Graphics:** Question cards as bold oversized text (existing CUT style). Add subtle card-counter icon (🂠 🂠) in mirror room frames — one disappears when each intervention is played. No other new graphics.
- **Edit pacing:** Scenes 1–2 cut briskly (CUT's signature rhythm). Scene 3 slows by ~20%. Scenes 4–5 let moments *breathe* — hold on faces 2–3 seconds longer than comfortable. Scene 6 is one continuous wide take with minimal cuts.
- **Sound design:** The pass-through slot sound (card sliding through) should be foley'd and slightly amplified — it's the most important non-verbal sound in the episode. Mirror room audio is slightly lower fidelity / more ambient than main set, reinforcing the separation.
- **Color grade:** Main set warm (existing CUT palette). Mirror room 15–20% cooler, slightly desaturated. When the two rooms appear in split-screen, the contrast should be visible but not cartoonish.
