## REVISED TITLE OPTIONS

**1. VULNERABILITY (REVISED)**
**"My Boyfriend Watched Me Play Truth or Drink With My Ex"**

Why it works: The title now accurately reflects the updated mechanic — it's the *new partner* watching, not the ex. "My Boyfriend" immediately establishes the stakes triangle: current relationship vs. past relationship, observed in real time. The word "watched" preserves the surveillance/voyeur dynamic from the original brief. "Play Truth or Drink" is format-recognizable (search + browse discoverability), while the first-person "My" maintains parasocial_score (LASSO-stable for ret_baseline and ret_above_50_pct). The tension is implicit: *what will she reveal, and what will he see?* This drives escalation_intensity and cliffhanger_score without needing explicit emotional language. The viewer instantly understands someone's current relationship is on the line.

---

**2. JEALOUSY ENGINE (NEW)**
**"Exes Play Truth or Drink. Their New Partners Hear Everything."**

Why it works: The period-separated structure creates a two-beat reveal — the first sentence is familiar (format recognition), the second sentence is the twist (pattern disruption). "Hear Everything" is more threatening than "are watching" because it implies no filter, no edit, no mercy. This activates cliffhanger_score and escalation_intensity simultaneously. The shift from first-person to third-person broadens the frame — this isn't one person's story, it's a *system* designed to produce conflict. "New Partners" is the critical payload: it introduces jealousy, insecurity, and relational threat. The viewer clicks to see the moment someone's current partner hears something they shouldn't.

---

**3. ASYMMETRIC POWER (NEW)**
**"They Don't Know Their Partners Are in the Next Room"**

Why it works: This is the pure surveillance hook — it foregrounds the information asymmetry that is the mechanic's core engine. "They Don't Know" triggers immediate dramatic irony; the viewer has information the subjects don't, which is one of the strongest retention drivers in reality/social content. "In the Next Room" adds physical proximity and spatial tension — the partners aren't abstract, they're *right there*. This title sacrifices format recognition (no "Truth or Drink") for raw curiosity gap, which maximizes cliffhanger_score (LASSO-stable for ret_above_50_pct, ret_stability, ret_initial_drop). Works best if the thumbnail clearly signals the Truth or Drink format.

---

**4. RELATIONSHIP ROULETTE (NEW)**
**"We Asked Exes Their Deepest Secrets — While Their Partners Listened"**

Why it works: "We Asked" positions CUT as the orchestrator/instigator, which is brand-consistent and activates the "social experiment" frame that performs well in browse. "Deepest Secrets" is high-valence emotional language driving escalation_intensity. The em-dash creates a syntactic pause before the reveal — "While Their Partners Listened" — which functions as a miniature cliffhanger within the title itself. "Listened" (vs. "watched") emphasizes the *content* of what's being revealed rather than the visual surveillance, implying that what's said is damaging enough that hearing it is the weapon. This balances format clarity with emotional stakes.

---

**5. THE MOMENT IT BREAKS (NEW)**
**"Playing Truth or Drink With My Ex Almost Ended My Relationship"**

Why it works: This is the consequence-forward title — it skips the mechanic and leads with the outcome. "Almost Ended My Relationship" is a high-stakes emotional claim that drives both click-through and retention (viewers stay to find *the moment*). First-person framing maximizes parasocial_score. "Almost" is doing critical work: it implies survival, which means the viewer gets both the crisis *and* the resolution — a complete emotional arc promised in the title. This pairs well with a thumbnail showing a visibly distressed current partner. Best used if the content delivers a genuine near-breaking-point moment.
