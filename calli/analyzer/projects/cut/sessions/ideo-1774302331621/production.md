## 1) SHOOTING SETUP & LOCATION

### Primary Set: "The Table" (Main Stage)

**Location:** CUT's in-house studio (or rented black-box studio, min. 30×40 ft to accommodate two isolated zones).

**Set Design:**
- **Round table, 36" diameter**, matte black or warm walnut finish — intimate enough that knees almost touch. Two simple mid-century chairs (mismatched on purpose — one warm leather, one velvet — signals that these two people are *different* now).
- **Backdrop:** CUT's signature seamless paper — but we're proposing a shift: **deep charcoal (not pure white)** to create a moodier, more emotionally charged frame. [[H14: dark-backdrop emotional engagement lift|Testing whether a darker backdrop increases perceived emotional intensity and watch-through rate vs. CUT's classic white]].
- **Table dressing:** One bottle of mid-shelf liquor (visible label cleared for brand), two shot glasses, and a small stack of question cards face-down. Nothing else. Negative space = tension.
- **Floor:** Black seamless carried onto the floor, or polished concrete if shooting in a loft space.

**Lighting Mood & Feel:**
- **Key light:** Soft, large-source overhead (4×4 silk with a Skypanel S60 above and slightly forward) creating a gentle downward wash — almost interrogation-room meets therapy-session.
- **Fill:** Minimal. We *want* shadows under the eyes and jawline. Ratio approx 3:1 key-to-fill.
- **Accent:** Two subtle warm-toned edge lights (Astera tubes or Quasar tubes, gelled to 1/4 CTO) on either side, raking across the shoulders to separate talent from the dark background.
- **Practical:** One small LED candle on the table (flicker mode) for texture in close-ups. Adds warmth without competing.
- **Overall feel:** Late-night conversation energy. Think: "2 AM at a bar after everyone else has left." Warm, slightly moody, confessional.

### Secondary Set: "The Monitor Room" (Hidden Partner Watch)

**Location:** Adjacent room or partitioned area within the same studio (must be fully sound-isolated from main set).

**Set Design:**
- Small couch or two director's chairs facing a **40" monitor** mounted on a stand or set into a simple black flat.
- Room is darker than the main set — lit primarily by the glow of the monitor itself, supplemented by a single soft overhead at very low intensity.
- Messy-casual dressing: a couple of drinks (non-alcoholic fine), maybe a blanket thrown over the couch arm. This space should feel like they're watching something they *shouldn't* be watching.

**Lighting Mood:**
- Motivated by monitor glow. A small Aputure MC panel hidden behind the monitor, set to cool white with slight blue shift, provides eye light.
- One soft top-down at ~20% for exposure floor.
- **Feel:** Voyeuristic. Surveillance cam meets reality TV confessional.

### Additional Coverage Notes:
- **GoPro / iPhone lockoff** on the table surface (low angle, looking up) as a texture/cutaway cam. Captures hands, pours, card flips. No operator needed — set and forget.
- All cameras shoot **4K, 24fps**, matched color profiles (S-Log3 or C-Log depending on bodies). This gives editorial flexibility for punch-ins and reframes in post.
- **Cam A and B** should be on the same side of the 180° line. Cam B can cross the line *only* for a specific dramatic beat (e.g., one person walks away or breaks the format).

### Audio:
- 4× lavalier mics (one per talent), wireless, recorded to a dedicated mixer/recorder.
- 1× overhead boom (just off-frame above the table) as a safety and for room tone.
- Room B gets its own stereo pair for ambient reactions (gasps, laughs, whispered commentary).

## 5) PACING & EDITING NOTES

### Structure & Pacing Map

| Segment | Duration (Edited) | Content | Pacing Notes |
|---------|-------------------|---------|-------------|
| **Cold Open** | 0:00–0:15 | The most shocking/funny moment from the video, cut mid-sentence. No context. Text card: "EXES: Truth or Drink." | **HOOK.** Must stop the scroll in <3 sec. Use the single most emotionally charged or absurd moment. [[H09: cold-open shock-cut vs. slow-build intro|Testing whether a decontextualized shock moment outperforms a slow-build character intro for 3-second retention]]. |
| **Introductions** | 0:15–0:55 | Jordan & Maya introduce themselves, explain their relationship history in 2–3 sentences each. Quick flash to Room B — partners wave, one-liner each. | Keep this TIGHT. Gen Z doesn't need lengthy backstory. Text overlays with names/ages/relationship duration handle exposition. |
| **Round 1: Warm-Up** | 0:55–2:00 | 2–3 lighter questions. "What's the first thing you noticed about me?" / "What's something you never told me while we were together?" | Build rapport on camera. Let the audience settle into the dynamic. Room B cutaways are brief — reaction shots only, no full answers. |
| **Round 2: Escalation** | 2:00–4:00 | 3–4 medium-intensity questions. "Did you ever cheat?" / "What did your friends really think of me?" Drinks start flowing. | **This is where the video earns its watch-time.** Increase Room B cutaway frequency. Hold on silence — don't cut away from discomfort. Let beats breathe 2–3 seconds longer than feels natural. |
| **Round 3: The Deep Cut** | 4:00–5:30 | 1–2 heavy questions. "Do you think we'd still be together if [X] hadn't happened?" / "Is your current partner better for you than I was?" | Maximum tension. Room B partners are now fully invested — their reactions become a parallel narrative. Use split-screen here if reactions are simultaneous and strong. |
| **Resolution / Outro** | 5:30–6:30 | Final moment: exes say one honest thing to each other. Partners enter the room (if format allows) or react to the final answer. | Emotional landing. Music fades in (lo-fi, warm, slightly melancholic). Don't over-edit — let the moment sit. End on a real human beat, not a punchline. |
| **End Card** | 6:30–6:45 | CUT logo, subscribe CTA, "Watch the FULL uncut version on [platform]" | Clean, fast, standard CUT branding. |

### Editing Philosophy:
- **Jump cuts are native language for Gen Z.** Don't over-smooth transitions. Hard cuts between cameras = energy.
- **Text overlays** for emphasis on key phrases (e.g., when someone says something jaw-dropping, pop the quote on screen in CUT's brand font). Use sparingly — max 5–7 per video.
- **Sound design:** Subtle but critical. A low bass hit or vinyl-scratch for drink moments. Room tone shifts between Set A and Room B to subconsciously signal location changes.
- **Split-screen:** Use for the 2–3 peak moments where the ex's answer and partner's reaction are equally compelling. Don't overuse — it loses power.
- **Color grade:** Warm, slightly desaturated. Main set should feel like a Safdie brothers palette — amber tones, deep shadows. Room B slightly cooler/bluer (monitor-lit).
- **Music:** Licensed lo-fi or ambient track. Underscore only — never competing with dialogue. Drops out entirely during the most intense exchanges (silence is louder).
- **Pacing target:** No single shot should hold longer than 8–10 seconds in the first 90 seconds. After the audience is hooked, shots can breathe longer (up to 15–20 sec for emotional beats in Round 3).

### Platform-Specific Cuts:
- **YouTube (primary):** 5:30–7:00 full edit.
- **TikTok/Reels/Shorts:** 3× vertical clips (45–90 sec each), extracted from peak moments. These are *separate edits*, not just crops — re-paced for vertical-first consumption with text overlays optimized for no-sound viewing. [[H22: vertical-first clip extraction sequencing|Testing whether releasing the most intense clip first vs. the funniest clip first drives more traffic back to the full YouTube video]].
- **Thumbnail:** Two exes mid-reaction (one laughing, one cringing) with bold text overlay. Bottle visible. Room B partners visible in a small inset or ghosted background.

| # | Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Contingency |

| 1 | **Talent emotional breakdown / walkout** — The format is inherently volatile. | 3 | **Room B audio/video feed failure** — Partners can't see or hear the main set, killing the reaction element. | **MEDIUM** | **HIGH** — Loses the entire B-plot of the video. | Redundant feed: primary HDMI run + backup via NDI/wireless transmitter. On set: director has **escalation cards** (harder questions) ready to deploy early if energy is flat. If the dynamic is truly dead after 15 minutes, director calls it and we pivot to a backup pair (see #6). |
| 5 | **Current partner reacts badly / causes conflict post-shoot** — A partner sees something that damages their relationship and blames CUT. | **MEDIUM** | **MEDIUM** — Reputational risk, potential legal/PR issue. | Pre-shoot consent process is exhaustive: partners are briefed that they *will* hear uncomfortable things. ## APPENDIX A: DETAILED SHOOTING SETUP & LOCATION

### Room A — The Table (Primary Set)

**Location Type:** Studio or converted warehouse space with controlled acoustics. Minimum 20' × 20' footprint to allow camera orbits and boom clearance. CUT's signature look is a **limbo void** — seamless background, no visible walls — so we either shoot against a **black cyc wall** or hang 12' × 20' duvetyne flats on speed rail to create a deep negative-fill cave.

**Set Design Notes:**
- **Table:** 30" round café table, matte black or raw wood (no gloss — kills reflections). Height matters: standard 30" dining height so elbows rest naturally and body language reads clearly.
- **Chairs:** Two simple stools or mid-century chairs — nothing with high backs that hide shoulders. We want full torso in frame for reaction reads. Seat height matched so neither participant looks down at the other.
- **Floor:** If concrete, leave raw. If not, lay matte black vinyl. No rugs (rolling chair/stool noise).
- **Negative space is the design.** The absence of decoration forces the viewer's eye onto faces, hands, and the bottle. This is intentional — [[H12:minimalist set increases perceived emotional intensity|the void amplifies every micro-expression]].

**Lighting Mood & Feel:**

| Element | Specification | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Key Light** | 1× Aputure 600d Pro through 4'×4' grid cloth, ¾ overhead, slightly favoring the gap between the two participants | Soft, even wrap on both faces without flat TV-interview look |
| **Fill / Negative Fill** | Floppy flags camera-left and camera-right to deepen shadows on the "away" cheek of each participant | Adds cinematic dimension; keeps it from looking like a podcast |
| **Hair / Separation** | 2× Aputure 300x with barn doors, gelled to warm tungsten (CTO ¼), rigged high and behind each participant | Lifts them off the black background, adds depth |
| **Practicals** | Small Edison-bulb pendant hung just above and behind the bottle (not in close-up frame, but visible in wide) | Gives the eye a warm anchor point; subconsciously "bar" feeling |
| **Accent** | 1× Astera Titan tube on floor behind talent, dimmed to 10%, warm amber | Subtle halo on the cyc to prevent the background from going to pure crushed black on lower-end screens |
| **Overall Mood** | Warm, intimate, slightly moody — think "2 AM conversation at a bar that's about to close" | Emotional honesty lighting. Not harsh, not glamorous. |

**Color Temperature Target:** 3800K–4200K mixed. Slightly warm of neutral. Grade in post to CUT's house LUT with lifted blacks and desaturated midtones.

### Room B — The Monitor Room (Partner Reactions)

**Location:** Adjacent room, ideally separated by at least one wall/hallway so audio doesn't bleed. Can be a green room, office, or even a large closet — the tighter and more claustrophobic, the better for reaction energy.

**Set Design Notes:**
- **Monitor:** 32"+ field monitor or consumer TV on a small table or wall-mounted. Feed must be LIVE with <1 sec latency (HDMI over Cat6 or SDI run, NOT wireless — too risky for dropouts).
- **Seating:** Couch or two chairs angled toward the monitor. Casual, living-room feel — we want them to forget they're being filmed.
- **Background:** Keep it simple. A textured wall, some warm practicals (a lamp, maybe some books). This room should feel "real" vs. Room A's stylized void — the contrast in post sells the concept.
- **Lighting:** Soft overhead LED panel (Aputure Nova P300c at low output, daylight balanced) plus the glow of the monitor itself as a motivated key light on their faces. Let the monitor light do work — it creates natural, dynamic lighting shifts as the screen content changes.

### Camera Strategy Philosophy

Three-camera minimum is non-negotiable. The conversational format means we can never call "cut" and re-shoot — every moment is unique. Camera A chases the emotional beat (whoever is reacting most powerfully). Camera B holds the wide as a safety and context shot. Camera C is our secret weapon — [[H07:partner reaction cutaways increase average view duration by 15-25%|the hidden partner reactions are what separate this from a standard Truth or Drink]].

**Sync:** All cameras jam-synced via timecode (Tentacle Sync units on each body). Audio scratch track on all cameras as backup. Clap slate at top of each "round" for redundancy.

**Media Management:** Dual-slot recording on all Sony bodies (CFexpress A + SD simultaneous backup). GoPro to single card but offloaded and backed up at wrap. DIT creates three copies: (1) edit drive, (2) on-site backup, (3) cloud upload initiated same day.

## APPENDIX E: PACING & EDITING NOTES

### Structure & Timing (5–7 Min Final Cut)

| Segment | Duration | Content | Editing Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cold Open** | 0:00–0:20 | The single most shocking/funny moment from the shoot, cut mid-sentence. Hard cut to black + title card. | No context. No names. Just the hook. Viewer has to stay to understand what they just saw. Test: if this clip doesn't make you say "WAIT WHAT" out loud, pick a different moment. |
| **Intro / Setup** | 0:20–0:50 | Title card: "EXES: Truth or Drink." Quick interstitial graphics explaining the format. Cut to Jordan and Maya sitting down, first seeing each other, nervous laughter. Introduce the bottle ritual. | Fast cuts. Use jump cuts within the intro to compress time. Lower-third names + relationship context ("Dated 2 years, broke up 8 months ago"). |
| **Round 1: Icebreakers** | 0:50–1:45 | 2–3 lighter questions. Establish chemistry, dynamic, humor. First partner reaction cutaway introduced here. | Let the first question breathe a little longer — the audience is still calibrating to these people. By Q2–Q3, cut tighter. FIRST partner cutaway should be a genuine surprise moment — the audience doesn't know the partners are watching yet (unless the cold open revealed it). |
| **Round 2: Escalation** | 1:45–3:30 | 3–4 medium-intensity questions. Jealousy, intimacy, unresolved feelings. More drinks poured. | Pacing accelerates. Use L-cuts and J-cuts to overlap audio from one camera angle with visuals from another — creates a subliminal feeling of mounting tension. Increase frequency of Room B cutaways. |
| **Round 3: The Deep End** | 3:30–5:30 | 2–3 high-intensity questions. The emotional peak. Tears, long silences, raw admissions. | **SLOW DOWN.** Let silences breathe. Hold on close-ups for 4–6 seconds without cutting — uncomfortable length that mirrors the discomfort on screen. Use the overhead cam for hands during long pauses. Partner reactions here should be intercut more aggressively — we're now cross-cutting a four-person emotional narrative. |
| **Cooldown / Outro** | 5:30–6:30 | Final question or a "is there anything you never got to say?" moment. Hug or handshake. Quick debrief soundbites. | Return to wider shots. Music fades in (warm, reflective lo-fi or indie track). Let the last moment land without a cut — hold the wide as they stand up, maybe embrace. |
| **End Card / CTA** | 6:30–7:00 | CUT Analytics end card. "Subscribe" CTA. Tease next video. | Standard branded template. Include a "watch the full uncut version on [platform]" if we're running an extended cut strategy. |

### Editing Style Guide

- **Jump cuts are our friend.** CUT's visual language is built on them. They compress dead air, create comedic timing, and feel native to Gen Z's consumption habits.
- **Sound design matters more than music.** The *clink* of shot glasses, the *thwack* of a card hitting the table, a sharp inhale before a confession — these are more powerful than any underscore. Music should be used sparingly: intro, transitions between rounds, and outro only.
- **Text overlays:** Use sparingly for context ("They dated for 2 years") or comedic emphasis (a word repeated in bold text when someone says something unhinged). Do NOT over-caption — this isn't a BuzzFeed listicle. [[H19:strategic text overlays on key quotes boost clip shareability on short-form platforms|Pop-up text on the 3–5 most quotable lines]] for social clipping purposes.
- **Color grade:** Warm, slightly desaturated, lifted blacks. Consistent across both rooms but Room B can skew slightly cooler (monitor light motivation) to visually differentiate the spaces.
- **Split-screen:** Use 2–3 times maximum for simultaneous ex + partner reactions. It's a powerful tool but loses impact if overused. Best deployed when a question directly references the current partner.
- **Aspect ratio:** Master in 16:9 for YouTube. Simultaneously flag moments for 9:16 reframing (vertical clips for TikTok/Shorts/Reels). The editor should deliver 5–8 vertical clips alongside the main cut.

### Audio Post

- Primary audio: Lav mix for dialogue clarity.
- Layer boom underneath for room tone and environmental texture.
- De-ess and de-breath lightly — we want it to sound human, not polished.
- Hard limiter on the master to catch sudden shouts or laughter spikes.

## APPENDIX F: KEY RISKS & CONTINGENCIES

| # | Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation / Contingency |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Talent emotional distress / breakdown beyond recoverable territory** | Medium | High — ethical liability, unusable footage, potential social media backlash if we publish someone in genuine crisis | Director has a pre-established **safe word** that any participant (including partners) can use to pause or stop filming. On-set PA trained in de-escalation. If a full stop is called, we have a 15-min cool-down protocol: cameras off, sound off, talent offered water and a private space. If they choose not to continue, we wrap gracefully and reshoot with backup talent if needed. We NEVER publish footage captured after a safe word is invoked. |
| 2 | **Current partner storms out of Room B or tries to enter Room A mid-shoot** | Low-Medium | High — breaks the format, potential confrontation on set, safety concern | Room B is physically separated (locked door or PA-guarded hallway). Partners are briefed pre-shoot: "You are observers. You will have a chance to talk to your partner after filming." If a partner becomes agitated, the Producer (stationed in/near Room B) intervenes immediately, offers a break, and assesses whether they can continue. Worst case: we lose the partner reaction footage but the main table content is still a viable video. |
| 3 | **Talent intoxication beyond usable/safe levels** | Medium | High — slurred speech = unusable audio, liability issues, duty of care | Shots are pre-poured at **half-standard size** (0.75 oz). Maximum 6 shots per person across the full shoot (~3 oz total = ~2 standard drinks over 45 min). PA tracks pours on a tally sheet. Water and snacks available. If talent appears impaired, Director pauses and pivots to "truth-only" for remaining questions. Craft services has food ready for immediate post-wrap consumption. |
| 4 | **Technical failure: live feed to Room B drops** | Medium | Medium — lose real-time partner reactions for that segment | Primary feed: hardwired HDMI-over-Cat6 extender. Backup: wireless HDMI transmitter (Hollyland Mars 400s) on hot standby. If both fail, Camera C operator pivots to showing partners the footage on a laptop with a 2–3 min delay (less ideal but salvageable). |
| 5 | **Camera / media failure** | Low | High — lost footage is unrecoverable in this format | Dual-slot recording on all primary cameras. Three-camera minimum means any single camera failure still leaves us with two usable angles. DIT spot-checks cards at the halfway mark (during any natural break). Backup camera body (A7S III) kitted and ready on a shelf. |
| 6 | **Talent backs out day-of** | Low-Medium | Very High — no show, no video | Maintain a **warm backup pair** (exes who completed casting but weren't selected as primary). They're on soft hold for shoot day — aware they may be called in. Offer a kill fee to primary talent in their contract to disincentivize no-shows ($150–$250). |
| 7 | **Content is flat — no emotional peaks, no memorable moments** | Medium | High — unusable or underperforming video | This is a casting problem, not a shoot-day problem. Mitigation lives upstream: rigorous casting process (see Casting Brief v2.0), pre-interviews that confirm unresolved tension, and a question script that escalates deliberately. On set, the Director has 5 "emergency" questions — provocative wildcards not on the printed cards — to deploy if energy is flagging. The Director also has latitude to go off-script with follow-up questions ("Wait — say more about that"). |
| 8 | **Post-publish talent regret / request to remove video** | Medium | High — takedown disrupts distribution, potential legal/PR issue | All talent sign a comprehensive release granting perpetual usage rights. HOWEVER, we also build in a **48-hour rough-cut review window** (T+7 to T+9) where talent can flag specific moments they're uncomfortable with. We are not obligated to remove them, but we genuinely consider each request. This goodwill policy protects our reputation with future talent pools. If a post-publish removal request comes in, it's escalated to legal and the Creative Director jointly. |
| 9 | **Alcohol licensing / platform compliance** | Low | Medium — video demonetized or age-gated on YouTube | Verify local permits for alcohol on a commercial shoot. YouTube: no glorification of excessive drinking. Our edit should frame drinking as a playful mechanic, not the point — the point is honesty. Avoid any shot of talent visibly drunk. Include a brief disclaimer card: "Please drink responsibly. All participants are 21+." TikTok clips should minimize visible alcohol to avoid suppression by the algorithm. |
| 10 | **Exes rekindle / current partners feel betrayed by the content** | Low-Medium | Medium — off-camera drama that could spill onto social media and reframe the video negatively | Pre-shoot briefing with ALL four participants (separately) sets expectations: "This is a conversation, not a reconciliation." Post-shoot, all four are offered a group debrief if desired. We cannot control what happens after they leave set, but we can ensure our production didn't manufacture a harmful outcome. |
