# Truth or Drink: Gen Z Edition — 4-Scene Storyboard

**Concept:** Two strangers (ages 20–25) sit across from each other at a minimalist table, asking increasingly personal questions about dating, mental health, money, and identity. They can answer honestly — or take a shot. The tension between vulnerability and avoidance *is* the content.

**Target Duration:** 5–7 minutes (edited)

---

## Scene 1: The Setup — "So… This Is Awkward"

**Visual Description:**
Wide two-shot on a 50mm lens, centered symmetry. The set is CUT's signature clean backdrop — matte black with a single warm overhead pendant light creating a soft pool on the table. Two clear shot glasses and a bottle of tequila sit between the participants. The mood is nervous energy; both participants fidget, laugh, and avoid eye contact. [[H01:test backdrop color on retention|We're testing whether a matte black backdrop outperforms CUT's classic white]] for Gen Z retention in the first 15 seconds.

**On-Screen Action & Key Dialogue:**
Participants introduce themselves with first names only. A title card pops up: *"They've never met. They have 20 questions. They can answer — or drink."* One participant says, "I already know I'm going to regret this," and the other laughs nervously, reaching for the bottle preemptively.

**Mood:** 😬 Nervous anticipation — the calm before the chaos. Light, bubbly, but with an undercurrent of "what did I sign up for?"

---

## Scene 2: The Warm-Up — "Easy… For Now"

**Visual Description:**
We shift to alternating medium close-ups (35mm, shallow depth of field) as each person reads from a question card. Lighting remains warm but we add a subtle fill light on the answering participant's face to catch micro-expressions. The edit pace is brisk — jump cuts trim dead air, keeping energy high. A soft lo-fi beat sits underneath at low volume.

**On-Screen Action & Key Dialogue:**
Questions escalate from playful ("What's your screen time average?") to mildly uncomfortable ("What's the most unhinged thing in your search history?"). One participant answers boldly — "Seven hours and forty-three minutes, and I'm not even ashamed" — while the other dodges the search history question and takes their first shot, grimacing. [[H02:test question pacing on engagement|We're testing whether showing 3 warm-up questions vs. 1 before the pivot affects mid-video retention]]. On-screen text graphics pop in with the question text in a bold sans-serif font (think: Instagram story aesthetic).

**Mood:** 😂 Playful escalation — laughs are genuine, defenses are still up, but cracks are forming. The audience leans in because they sense what's coming.

---

## Scene 3: The Pivot — "Okay, That One Hurts"

**Visual Description:**
Camera tightens to tight close-ups — eyes, hands gripping the shot glass, a lip bite before answering. The lo-fi music drops out entirely; we hear only room tone and voices. Lighting shifts subtly cooler (we pull the warm gel back 20%) to signal a tonal change. The edit slows down — we let silences breathe for 2–4 seconds, which is an eternity in short-form content. This is the emotional core.

**On-Screen Action & Key Dialogue:**
The question card reads: *"What's something you've never told your parents?"* A long pause. One participant's eyes glass over. They say, "I, um… I've been in therapy for two years and they don't know." The other participant sets down their shot glass untouched and responds quietly: "Mine is that I almost didn't make it through 2021." No one drinks. The camera holds. A beat of silence, then one reaches across the table and squeezes the other's hand. [[H03:test vulnerability moment length on shares|We're testing whether holding the emotional beat for 5 seconds vs. 10 seconds drives higher share rate on TikTok and YouTube Shorts clips]].

**Mood:** 🥺 Raw vulnerability — the mask drops completely. This is the moment the audience screenshots, clips, and sends to a friend with "this broke me." The silence does more work than any dialogue.

---

## Scene 4: The Release — "I'm Glad I Didn't Just Drink"

**Visual Description:**
We return to the wide two-shot from Scene 1, completing the visual bookend. Warm lighting is restored — even slightly warmer than the opening, signaling emotional resolution. Both participants are visibly looser: leaning in, laughing, shoulders dropped. The bottle is noticeably lower. We add a subtle lens flare from the pendant light to give the final frames a golden, almost nostalgic quality.

**On-Screen Action & Key Dialogue:**
Final question (read aloud together from the same card): *"Was it worth it?"* Both answer simultaneously — one says "Absolutely not" while the other says "One hundred percent" — and they burst out laughing. They cheers with their last shots. End card fades in: *"Truth or Drink — New episodes every Thursday."* A 3-second blooper reel tag plays under the subscribe prompt: one participant snort-laughing mid-sip.

**Mood:** 😊 Warm catharsis — the emotional payoff. Viewers feel like they just witnessed a real human connection form in real time. Relief, warmth, and the urge to rewatch.

---

## Director's Note

**Pacing:** The video follows a classic emotional arc — **tension → play → vulnerability → release** — compressed into ~6 minutes. Scenes 1 and 2 should move fast (jump cuts, 1.5–2 min combined). Scene 3 is the anchor and should be given room to breathe (2–2.5 min). Scene 4 is the exhale (45–60 sec). The ratio of lighthearted to emotional content should sit around 60/40 — enough levity to earn the gut-punch.

**The Clip:** Scene 3 is the clip. Specifically, the "I almost didn't make it through 2021" moment, cut as a 45–60 second vertical extract with the question card as a text overlay hook in the first frame. This is the moment engineered for TikTok/Reels/Shorts virality — it leads with tension (the pause), delivers an emotional payload (the confession), and ends on a human gesture (the hand squeeze) that demands a comment or share. Pre-roll text: *"She said the thing nobody expected…"*

**Hypothesis Summary:**
- **[[H01]]** — Backdrop color A/B test (black vs. white) measuring 15-second retention
- **[[H02]]** — Warm-up question quantity test (3 vs. 1) measuring mid-video drop-off
- **[[H03]]** — Vulnerability beat duration test (5s vs. 10s hold) measuring share/save rate on short-form clips

These three hypotheses should be tested in the first two upload cycles (Week 1: H01 via thumbnail/backdrop swap on the long-form cut; Week 2: H02 and H03 via two alternate edits of the short-form clip). Results feed directly into the production template for Episode 2.

## Appendix: Hypothesis Detail Links

### [[H01: Backdrop Color A/B Test | "the stark black void behind them"]]

- **What we're testing:** Does a black seamless backdrop outperform a white/cream backdrop on 15-second retention rate for the long-form YouTube upload?
- **Why it matters:** Gen Z content trends show high engagement with both "dark aesthetic" (moody, intimate) and "clean girl" (bright, minimal) visual languages. We're assuming black = intimacy = longer watch, but the data may say otherwise.
- **Method:** Shoot both backdrops in the same session (first half of questions on black, second half on white — then cut two alternate full-length versions). Publish as an A/B test via YouTube's "Test & Compare" thumbnail feature or as two unlisted links distributed 50/50 through paid promotion.
- **Success metric:** ≥8% lift in 15-second retention for the winning variant.
- **Fallback:** If results are statistically insignificant (<3% delta), default to black for brand consistency and re-test with a more dramatic variable (e.g., colored gel lighting vs. neutral).

---

### [[H02: Warm-Up Question Quantity Test | "three rapid-fire softballs"]]

- **What we're testing:** Does including 3 warm-up questions (Scene 2) versus jumping in after just 1 warm-up reduce mid-video drop-off (measured at the 2:00–2:30 mark)?
- **Why it matters:** Warm-ups build chemistry and give viewers a reason to invest in the participants — but they also delay the "good stuff." Gen Z audiences on YouTube have notoriously low patience for preamble. We need to find the minimum viable warm-up.
- **Method:** Cut two versions of the full video — one with the full 3-question warm-up sequence, one trimmed to a single warm-up question with a faster transition into Scene 3. Distribute via two separate uploads one week apart (or A/B through a split-traffic tool).
- **Success metric:** The winning version shows ≤12% audience drop-off at the 2:00–2:30 timestamp (per YouTube Analytics retention graph).
- **Fallback:** If 1 warm-up wins, future episodes adopt a "cold open → single warm-up → deep dive" structure. If 3 wins, we keep the current arc but tighten each warm-up to under 20 seconds.

---

### [[H03: Vulnerability Beat Duration Test | "five full seconds of silence"]]

- **What we're testing:** In the short-form clip extracted from Scene 3, does holding the silence/reaction beat for ~10 seconds outperform a ~5-second hold on share rate and save rate across TikTok, Reels, and Shorts?
- **Why it matters:** The pause is the emotional engine of the clip. Too short and it feels rushed; too long and the algorithm may read it as dead air and suppress distribution. This is the single highest-leverage edit decision for virality.
- **Method:** Export two versions of the 45–60 second clip — identical except for the duration of the silence after the vulnerability confession. Post Version A (5s) on TikTok and Reels simultaneously; post Version B (10s) on the same platforms 48 hours later (or vice versa, randomized). Compare 7-day share and save metrics normalized for impression count.
- **Success metric:** ≥15% lift in combined share + save rate per 1,000 impressions for the winning variant.
- **Fallback:** If neither version significantly outperforms, default to 7 seconds (the midpoint) and shift testing focus to the text overlay hook instead (*"She said the thing nobody expected…"* vs. *"Watch until the end"* vs. no text).

## Director's Note

**Pacing Philosophy:** This video lives and dies on rhythm — specifically, the contrast between comedic acceleration and emotional deceleration. Scenes 1 and 2 should feel like a rollercoaster climbing: quick cuts, overlapping laughter, energy building. Scene 3 is the drop — we slow everything down, let the camera linger, and trust the silence. Scene 4 is the gentle coast back to the platform, warm and resolved. Total runtime target: **5:30–6:30** in final cut.

**The Clip:** Scene 3 is unequivocally the viral clip. The moment a participant chooses to answer a deep-cut question instead of drinking — and the raw, unfiltered confession that follows — is where this video earns its emotional currency. We protect this moment in the edit by:
- Cutting to the tight B-cam close-up *before* the answer begins (so the audience is already locked in)
- Letting the full answer play without music, jump cuts, or reaction inserts
- Holding on the other participant's face for [[H02:silence duration after confession|a sustained beat of silence]] after the confession lands
- Only *then* allowing the score to creep back in underneath

**Short-Form Extraction Strategy:** The 45–60 second clip should begin 3 seconds *before* the question is read aloud (to build anticipation), include the full confession, and end on the other participant's wordless reaction. The hook text overlay — tested per H02's fallback protocol — appears in the first 1.5 seconds. We cut three variants: one for TikTok (9:16, subtitled, text hook), one for Reels (9:16, subtitled, no text hook for A/B comparison), and one for Shorts (9:16, slightly longer with 5 seconds of Scene 4 warmth appended as a resolution tag to boost completion rate).

**Energy Management on Set:** I'll direct the participants conversationally between takes, never letting the energy fully drop between scenes. Between Scene 2 and Scene 3, I'll call a soft "reset" — refill drinks, dim the lights slightly, and privately tell each participant: *"The next few questions are heavier. You never have to answer anything. The drink is always there. But if something feels true, trust it."* This primes vulnerability without manufacturing it.

**Final Instinct:** The best Truth or Drink videos don't go viral because of the questions. They go viral because of the *micro-moment* between hearing the question and deciding what to do — that two-second window where you watch someone's face calculate honesty against self-protection. Every camera angle, every lighting choice, every edit decision in this storyboard is designed to make that two-second window feel like the most important moment on the internet.
