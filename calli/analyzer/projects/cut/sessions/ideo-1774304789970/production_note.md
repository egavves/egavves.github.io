## Concept

Two exes sit across from each other at a table loaded with shots — they broke up months ago, but neither has changed their shared passwords to Netflix, Venmo, Spotify, even their phone. The premise is simple: answer the question honestly, or drink. **But** every round, the host reveals real data pulled from their shared accounts — Venmo transactions, Spotify listening history, Netflix watch activity — so lying isn't just risky, it's provable. The format weaponizes digital intimacy: in 2024, sharing a password is more vulnerable than sharing a bed.

**The Hook:** The screen flashes a Venmo transaction — "$42 — 🍕👀" sent at 2:14 AM to someone named "Jordan." One ex's face drops. The other hasn't seen it yet. You cannot scroll past the moment someone's digital footprint betrays them in real time.

**The WTF Moment:** In Round 4, the host slides a phone across the table showing Mia's Spotify "On Repeat" playlist — it's entirely songs from the playlist Caleb made her when they were dating. Mia goes dead silent. Caleb whispers, "You still listen to that?" Mia picks up the shot glass, then puts it down and says, "Every night before bed." The table erupts. [[H01:Test cold open featuring Mia's Spotify reveal at 0:04 vs Caleb's Venmo receipt reveal at 0:04 — measure ret_initial_drop over first 48hrs|This is the clip that breaks TikTok.]]

## Cast

**Mia — The One Who Still Cares (Hero)**
25, graphic designer, broke up with Caleb four months ago after he got a job offer in another city. She pretends she's moved on — posts thirst traps, goes to hot girl brunches — but her digital life tells a different story. Emotionally articulate when she finally drops the armor.
**Reveal:** She's been rewatching their shared Netflix list every Sunday, the exact night they used to have date night.

**Caleb — The Avoidant Who Feels Everything (Villain / Reluctant Vulnerability)**
27, software engineer, initiated the breakup but never actually deleted anything. Uses humor as deflection. Comes across as unbothered until the data makes it impossible. His arc is the slow crumble of the "I'm fine" facade.
**Reveal:** He's been sending Mia micro-payments on Venmo for their old inside jokes — $1 with a taco emoji — at 2 AM, then deleting them. The host has the receipts.

**Deja — The Best Friend Witness (Chaotic Wildcard / Super Fun)**
24, Mia's roommate who was invited as "emotional support" but immediately becomes the audience surrogate. She gasps, she narrates, she asks the questions the audience is screaming. She has zero filter and maximum investment.
**Reveal:** She's the one who told production about the passwords in the first place.

**Kofi — The Neutral Party (Underdog)**
26, Caleb's college friend, dragged here against his will. Quiet, observant, drops devastating one-liners. He's the moral compass — when he reacts, the audience knows something real just happened.
**Reveal:** He tells Caleb, on camera, "Bro, you talk about her every time you're drunk. Just say it."

**Chemistry & Group Dynamic:** Mia and Caleb have the gravitational pull of two people who are clearly not over each other but have built elaborate emotional scaffolding to pretend otherwise. Deja and Kofi function as the Greek chorus — Deja is chaotic energy and gasoline, Kofi is the match that lights the real moments with quiet truth. The four-person dynamic prevents the exes from retreating into polite small talk; there's always someone to escalate or puncture the tension.

**Character Archetypes:**
- **Hero:** Mia — the audience roots for her honesty
- **Villain:** Caleb — creates friction through avoidance and deflection
- **Underdog:** Kofi — underestimated, delivers the emotional kill shot
- **Chaotic Wildcard / Super Fun:** Deja — entertainment engine, reaction gold
- **Heartwarming (in ending):** Caleb — when he finally breaks, it lands because he resisted so long

## The Loop

**Core Loop:** Host asks question → ex must answer truthfully or drink → host reveals digital evidence that confirms or contradicts → group reacts and the emotional stakes compound

**Loop Family:** Truth/Reveal + Pressure

**Why This Loop Fits:** The Truth/Reveal loop is the backbone of Truth or Drink, but layering in verifiable digital evidence transforms it from a conversation format into a reveal format. Each round has a built-in [[H02:Test rounds with digital evidence reveals vs rounds with questions only — measure peaks_per_minute and ret_stability over first 72hrs|binary surprise mechanic]] — the participant doesn't know what data the host has, so every answer carries the risk of being publicly exposed.

**Secondary Loops:**
- Evidence tease → participant anxiety → reveal → emotional fallout
- Friend reaction → pressure on ex → confession or deflection → friend escalates

**Currency of tension:** Emotional honesty. Every drink is a visible admission that the truth is too painful to say out loud. Every answer risks exposing feelings the person has been hiding — not just from their ex, but from themselves.

**Ramp:** Hybrid staircase/tightrope. Questions escalate from playful ("Have you stalked their profile this week?") to devastating ("Do you regret the breakup?"). The digital evidence escalates in intimacy — from Netflix history to Venmo to the actual text drafts they never sent. [[H03:Test 5-round structure with linear escalation vs 7-round structure with mid-video cool-down beat — measure ret_end_ratio and payoff_satisfaction_score over first 48hrs|The tightrope element]] comes from not knowing when the most damaging evidence will drop.

**Payoff:** Are they getting back together — or is this the real goodbye?

## Psychological Drivers

**Primary Drivers:**
- **Vulnerability:** Every round forces participants to choose between emotional exposure and the visible shame of drinking — the audience feels the weight of that choice in their chest.
- **Prediction tension:** Viewers are constantly guessing: will they answer or drink? Is the evidence going to confirm what we suspect? The digital reveal adds a second prediction layer on top of the classic Truth or Drink mechanic.
- **Rejection anxiety:** The entire format is built on the terror of admitting you still care about someone who might not care back — a universal fear that keeps viewers locked in.
- **Social risk:** These aren't anonymous confessions; friends are present, cameras are rolling, and digital receipts are permanent. The social cost of honesty is visible and real.

**Secondary Drivers:**
- **Second-hand embarrassment:** When Caleb's 2 AM Venmo transactions get exposed, viewers physically cringe — this is the engine of shareability.
- **Authenticity verification:** The digital evidence creates a lie-detector effect; viewers are watching to see if someone's self-presentation matches their actual behavior.
- **Attraction uncertainty:** The exes' body language, micro-expressions, and involuntary smiles create a "will they or won't they" tension that's irresistible.
- **Nostalgia identification:** Anyone who's ever kept an ex's hoodie, playlist, or password will see themselves in this — it activates personal memory and emotional projection.
- **Parasocial investment:** By the midpoint, the audience has picked a side and is emotionally committed to a specific outcome.

## Emotional Design

**Tone:** Warm but unflinching. This isn't a roast — it's an excavation. The lighting is soft, the music is lo-fi, but the questions are surgical.

**Dominant Emotions:** Nostalgia, vulnerability, second-hand embarrassment, tenderness, surprise.

**Emotional Arc:** Playful discomfort → escalating exposure → emotional breakthrough → bittersweet resolution. The arc mirrors the five stages of a breakup in reverse: denial (early deflection), anger (Mia when she sees the Venmo), bargaining (Caleb's humor), depression (the quiet middle), acceptance (the ending).

**Tension Source:** The gap between what people say and what their digital behavior proves. Every round is a potential lie-detector moment.

**Humor:** Deja is the humor engine — her reactions provide levity between heavy moments. Caleb's early-round deflection humor also serves this function, but it becomes poignant as it stops working. **Camera:** Three cameras — two on the exes (medium close-ups, shallow depth of field), one wide for group reactions. **Graphics:** Clean lower thirds with name/relationship status. Digital evidence displayed as phone screen mockups overlaid in post — stylized but readable. Round numbers appear as subtle text transitions.

**Testimonials:** No talking heads. All reactions are in-the-moment. Post-roll card: "Mia and Caleb still haven't changed their passwords. It's been 3 weeks."

## Structural Fragility

1. **Casting failure:** If the exes are genuinely over each other, the digital evidence reveals land flat instead of devastating. The entire format depends on unresolved feelings being real. Pre-interviews must verify ongoing emotional attachment and active account usage.

2. **Evidence quality variance:** Not every couple will have equally dramatic digital footprints. ## Top Strengths / Problems / Fixes

**Top 3 Strengths:**
1. **The evidence mechanic is a format innovation** — it solves Truth or Drink's biggest weakness (people can just lie) by introducing verifiable data, creating a reveal-driven loop on top of a conversation format.
2. **Universal relatability** — password sharing is a generational intimacy marker that virtually every Gen Z viewer has experienced; the premise requires zero explanation and triggers immediate self-projection.
3. **Built-in emotional arc** — the format naturally escalates from playful to devastating, with a clear climax (the avoidant partner breaking) and a satisfying but open-ended resolution.

**Top 3 Problems:**
1. **Casting dependency is extreme** — the format needs exes who are genuinely unresolved AND have interesting digital footprints AND have expressive friends willing to participate.
2. **Privacy/consent complexity** — accessing and displaying real account data requires careful legal and ethical navigation; participants must fully consent to specific data being shown.
3. **Repeatability ceiling** — the "passwords" premise is specific; scaling to a series requires either broadening the digital intimacy concept or finding new relationship dynamics.

**Fix Engine:**
1. **Build a casting pipeline with a digital audit step:** Require potential participants to submit screenshots of shared account activity during the application process. Score the evidence for dramatic potential before scheduling shoots.
2. **Create a "data consent" protocol:** Participants pre-approve specific data points (Venmo transactions, Spotify playlists, Netflix history) in advance. The "surprise" is that it's being shown to their ex, not that it exists. This protects the channel legally and maintains the reveal mechanic.
3. **Develop a "Digital Intimacy" series umbrella:** [[H14:Test 'We Still Have Each Other's Passwords' as a standalone vs first episode in a 'Digital Intimacy' series including 'We Still Share a Spotify' and 'We Still Have Each Other's Location' — measure traffic_subscriber_pct and views_public across 3 episodes|Expand the concept]] to "We Still Share Location," "We Never Deleted the Photos," "We Still Have the Group Chat" — each episode explores a different digital intimacy artifact with the same Truth or Drink + evidence structure.
4. **Cast friends as mandatory, not optional:** The four-person format is non-negotiable. Solo exes will collapse into therapy. The friends provide levity, escalation, and reaction content that sustains the format between evidence drops.
