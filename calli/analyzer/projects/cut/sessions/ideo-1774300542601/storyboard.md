# PRODUCTION NOTE: "Exes Play Truth or Drink… But On — Storyboard

# CUT Analytics Storyboard: "Truth or Drink — But One Couple Doesn't Know They're Both Here"

**Format:** Truth or Drink · **Target:** Gen Z (18–25) · **Duration:** 5–7 min · **Participants:** 6 recent post-breakup singles, including one secretly reunited ex-couple — neither knows the other was cast

---

## SCENE 1: "The Setup"

**Duration:** 0:00–0:45

**Visual Description:**
Wide establishing shot on a 50mm lens slowly dollies in to a minimal **long communal table** set against a desaturated sage-green backdrop — CUT's signature clean aesthetic but warmed up with soft, diffused overhead key light (3200K) and a subtle halo edge light behind each participant. The set dressing is sparse: **six chairs around a single shared table** holding a bottle of tequila and **six** shot glasses. Mood is inviting but carries a nervous electricity — think "first day of school meets late-night confessional." **A wider frame than typical CUT setups — the camera needs room to hold all six faces, but especially the spatial relationship between the two exes seated directly across from each other.**

**On-Screen Action & Key Dialogue:**
Six strangers (pre-cast: **four genuine strangers plus one secretly reunited ex-couple, separated during arrival so neither sees the other before cameras roll**) sit down in a staggered sequence. They introduce themselves with first names and one "unexpected fact." **We hold on the two exes' faces the moment they clock each other — the recognition beat is captured on a dedicated locked-off 85mm reaction cam per ex, running continuously from the moment participants enter frame.** Title card animates on: **"Six strangers are playing Truth or Drink. But one couple doesn't know they're both here."** The audience is in on the secret before anyone at the table fully processes it. Nervous laughter fills the gap — **but two of the laughs land differently.**

**Mood:** 😬 Awkward Anticipation — the audience should feel the tension of strangers sizing each other up, **compounded by the dramatic irony of watching two people realize in real time that this isn't what they signed up for** — leaning into the cringe-curiosity loop that drives Gen Z engagement. **The information asymmetry is the hook: we know, they're just finding out.**

---

## SCENE 2: "The Warm-Up Round"

**Duration:** 0:45–2:30

**Visual Description:**
Camera shifts to alternating medium close-ups (85mm, shallow depth of field) to capture micro-expressions — every eye-dart, lip-bite, and suppressed smile. **The edit intercuts between the wider group dynamic and the dedicated ex-couple reaction cams, establishing a visual grammar: group shots feel like a normal Truth or Drink; the tight two-shots and singles on the exes feel like a secret documentary happening inside the game.** Lighting stays warm but we add a subtle practical light glow from underneath the shot glasses (a small LED strip beneath frosted glass) to draw the eye to the "drink or answer" decision point. The edit rhythm here is quick: jump cuts on reactions, inserting tight reaction-cam cutaways every 8–12 seconds — **with priority given to moments where an ex's answer visibly lands on the other ex's face.**

**On-Screen Action & Key Dialogue:**
Questions are posed to the full table and escalate from soft ("What's the most embarrassing thing on your screen time report?") to medium ("What's the worst lie you ever told a partner — and did it work?"). **The questions are designed to feel generic to the four true strangers but land with surgical specificity on the exes.** One participant **among the strangers** deflects with humor and drinks instead of answering — the table erupts. **Meanwhile, one of the exes answers the "worst lie" question honestly, and the other ex's jaw tightens almost imperceptibly. The dedicated reaction cam catches it. The four strangers don't notice yet. The audience does.** The rhythm of "answer or drink" trains the viewer on the format's stakes: **vulnerability is the currency, but for two people at this table, the cost is exponentially higher.**

**Mood:** 🫠 Loosening Up / Simmering Tension — the surface reads as six people warming to each other; the subtext, visible only through the editorial emphasis on the exes, is a slow-building pressure cooker. **The audience holds two realities simultaneously — the game everyone thinks they're playing, and the game that's actually happening.**

---

## SCENE 3: "The Narrowing"

**Duration:** 2:30–4:30

**Visual Description:**
**The lighting subtly shifts — the warm overhead key remains, but we introduce a cooler fill (4500K) that creeps in as the questions deepen, creating a barely perceptible tonal change the audience feels before they identify.** Camera work tightens: we move from medium close-ups to **true close-ups on the exes, the depth of field razor-thin, isolating them from the group.** The 50mm wide shot now serves as a "reset" angle between questions, letting the audience see the full table geography — **who's leaning in, who's leaning back, who can't stop glancing across the table.** Jump-cut rhythm slows to let moments breathe: 15–20 second holds on faces during silence.

**On-Screen Action & Key Dialogue:**
Questions narrow from general to pointed: "Have you ever been the toxic one in a relationship?" → "What's something you never apologized for but should have?" → **"If your most recent ex were sitting across from you right now, what would you say to them?"** The table laughs at the hypothetical. **The exes do not laugh.** One of the four strangers starts to notice the energy shift — a glance between the exes, a too-long pause. **The question hangs in the air. One ex drinks. The other answers.** What they say (unscripted, real) becomes the emotional axis of the entire piece. **A stranger asks, half-joking: "Wait… do you two know each other?"** The room pivots.

**Mood:** 😶 The Penny Dropping — this is the scene the algorithm will clip. The dramatic irony collapses into shared reality. The audience's privileged knowledge becomes the table's lived experience. **Tension converts to raw, unscripted confrontation-or-connection.**

---

## SCENE 4: "The Reveal / The Real Conversation"

**Duration:** 4:30–6:00

**Visual Description:**
**We cut the jump-cut rhythm entirely. The camera holds — long, unbroken takes on a slow-pushing 50mm, treating the moment with documentary gravity.** The cool fill light now matches the key in intensity; the room feels more honest, less produced. **The four strangers are still in frame but the depth of field and editorial focus make them soft background witnesses — a Greek chorus.** The dedicated ex-couple reaction cams become the primary footage source. **If one ex cries, we don't cut away. If they laugh, we stay. The edit trusts the moment.**

**On-Screen Action & Key Dialogue:**
The exes are given the choice: leave, stay and keep playing with the group, or **ask each other one question directly — truth or drink, just the two of them, while the table watches.** This is unscripted. Whatever they choose is the scene. **If they engage, the question they ask each other is the emotional climax. If they refuse, the refusal itself is the story.** The strangers' reactions — disbelief, empathy, secondhand heartbreak — are captured as B-roll gold. **One stranger, quietly: "I did not sign up for this." (Neither did they.)**

**Mood:** 💔/🫂 Catharsis or Collision — the audience doesn't know which they'll get, and neither do the participants. This is the payoff of the information asymmetry: **the secret isn't a gimmick, it's the mechanism that produces a moment no scripted content can replicate.**

---

## SCENE 5: "The Exhale"

**Duration:** 6:00–6:45

**Visual Description:**
**Return to the wide 50mm, now slowly dollying back out — reversing the opening move.** Lighting warms back to 3200K. The table is messier now: shot glasses empty or tipped, body language completely transformed from Scene 1. **A split-screen moment: the exes' faces in Scene 1 (strangers discovering each other) vs. now.** The sage-green backdrop feels softer. The halo edge lights catch any remaining emotion on faces.

**On-Screen Action & Key Dialogue:**
All six participants get a closing moment. The strangers share brief reactions ("That was the most intense thing I've ever witnessed"). **The exes get the final word — separately, in individual interview-style pickups shot immediately after, while the emotion is still raw.** No leading questions from producers. Just: "Is there anything else you want to say?" **Title card: "They hadn't spoken in [X] months. Neither knew the other would be here."** Hard cut to black. **CUT logo. No music over the final 3 seconds — just room tone.**

**Mood:** 🫠➡️🥹 Release — the tension that's been building since the first frame finally exhales. The audience is left sitting with the same unresolved, complicated feeling the exes are. **This is the comment-section fuel: "I need a part 2," "the way he looked at her when she answered," "I'm literally shaking." The asymmetry delivered.**

### EDITORIAL & POST-PRODUCTION NOTES:

- **The information asymmetry is the spine of every editorial decision.** The audience knows before the table knows. Every cut, every held frame, every music cue should widen or collapse that gap deliberately.
- **Music:** Minimal, low-frequency ambient tone (à la A24 tension scoring) through Scenes 1–3. **Drops entirely in Scene 4.** Returns as a warm, resolving pad in Scene 5.
- **Captioning/Text overlays:** CUT's standard lower-thirds for names. **Add a subtle persistent visual cue (a small icon or color-coded name tag) so the audience can always track the exes in group shots without heavy-handed labeling.**
- **The four strangers are not extras — they're the audience surrogates.** Cast for emotional intelligence and genuine reactivity. Their dawning realization mirrors the viewer's satisfaction at being "in on it."
- **Ethical guardrail:** Both exes signed releases with full knowledge that "another participant may be someone from your past." They consented to the possibility without being told the certainty. **A producer is on standby off-camera with authority to cut if either participant signals distress.** This is the line between compelling content and exploitation — and we stay on the right side of it.
