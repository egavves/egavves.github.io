## REVISED TITLE OPTIONS

**1. VULNERABILITY (REPLACED)**
~~"Gen Z Strangers Play Truth or Drink… But One Person Has a Secret"~~

**→ "Exes Play Truth or Drink… But One Couple Doesn't Know They're Both Here"**

Why it works: This is the strongest possible articulation of the information asymmetry mechanic that every test respondent cited as the click driver. The title makes the viewer omniscient — *you* know something the participants don't — which is the purest form of title_fomo_score optimization (LASSO-stable for views_public, ret_baseline, ret_above_50_pct, and outlier_retention_score). "Doesn't Know They're Both Here" is a concrete, falsifiable claim that creates an immediate open loop with a guaranteed payoff moment (the recognition/reveal). Unlike the v2 "secret" framing, this doesn't dilute the asymmetry into generic mystery — it names the *exact structural tension* of the episode. The cliffhanger_score benefit (LASSO-stable for ret_stability, ret_30s, ret_initial_drop) is also stronger because viewers aren't waiting for *a* secret to drop — they're scanning every interaction for the moment two specific people realize they're sitting across from their ex.

**2. STAKES** *(Keep if previously strong — provide current title for evaluation)*

**"6 Strangers Play Truth or Drink. 2 of Them Used to Date."**

Why it works: Clean, declarative, high-contrast structure. The period after "Truth or Drink" creates a hard stop that resets attention before the reveal clause. "Used to date" is lower-temperature than "exes" which paradoxically increases curiosity — it sounds like discovered information, not a produced setup. This frames the video as observational rather than manufactured, boosting authenticity_score. The numeric specificity ("6… 2") gives the thumbnail/title pairing concrete visual math that viewers process instantly.

**3. CLIFFHANGER (REPLACED/UPGRADED)**

**"They Sat Down With Strangers… But One Person Wasn't a Stranger"**

Why it works: This inverts the POV to the participants' perspective, creating empathic tension rather than omniscient tension (complementing Option 1). "Wasn't a Stranger" is a soft contradiction that forces a double-take — the brain has to reconcile "strangers" with "wasn't a stranger," which increases dwell time on the title itself. The past tense ("Sat Down") subtly signals that something has *already happened*, activating narrative curiosity rather than premise curiosity. Drives cliffhanger_score and ret_initial_drop resistance because the viewer enters already mid-story.

**4. SOCIAL PROOF / REACTION BAIT**

**"Gen Z Plays Truth or Drink… But Two Players Don't Know They Dated Each Other's Ex"**

*FLAGGED FOR CUT: This is a deliberate misdirection variant.* If the actual concept is that two people are *each other's* exes (not that they dated each other's ex), discard this. But if the table includes overlapping relationship webs beyond the central couple, this title widens the asymmetry web and increases rewatch value. Drives shares_per_view because it implies a web of connections viewers will want to diagram/discuss.

**5. MINIMALIST / ALGORITHM HEDGE**

**"Truth or Drink, But Two of Them Are Exes"**

Why it works: Shortest possible version. Zero wasted words. Relies entirely on CUT's brand recognition to carry the "strangers at a table" context — anyone who's seen one CUT video fills in the entire visual frame automatically. The "But" pivot is doing all the work. This is the insurance title: if the algorithm is compressing title display or if mobile truncation is cutting longer options, this version survives intact. Every word is load-bearing.
