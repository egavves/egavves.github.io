# PART I: CASTING BRIEF

## Demographics & Energy

**Age Range:** 20–24 (core Gen Z sweet spot — old enough for alcohol on camera, young enough to still feel the stakes of vulnerability questions)

**Personality Types We're Hunting:**

We need people who live in the space between "chronically online" and "emotionally literate." Gen Z doesn't reward polish — they reward *realness that feels specific.* We want participants who:

- Process emotions out loud rather than performing them
- Default to humor as a coping mechanism but can drop it when something lands
- Have a natural rhythm of interrupting, riffing, and circling back — the conversational cadence of group chats made verbal
- Carry opinions they haven't fully figured out yet and are okay saying so

**Diversity (Non-Negotiable):**

Racial, ethnic, body type, gender expression, neurodivergence, socioeconomic background, regional accent. Not as a checklist — as a *texture.* The room should feel like a real friend group, not a stock photo. We specifically want to avoid the "LA creative class monoculture" that plagues similar formats. [[H01: Audience retention increases 18-22% when cast diversity reflects viewer self-identification rather than aspirational identity|diversity as mirror not window]].

---

## Character Archetypes Needed

For a two-person episode (JESS & MARCO), we're collapsing six archetypes into a dynamic pairing:

| Archetype | How It Manifests | Casting Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Hero** | The one the audience roots for — not because they're perfect, but because they're *trying* | Should emerge organically. Whoever answers the hardest question without drinking becomes the Hero. Cast someone capable of earning this. |
| **Villain** | Not malicious. The one who asks the follow-up question the AUDIENCE was thinking. The instigator. | We need someone who can push without being cruel. Playful antagonism, not toxicity. |
| **Underdog** | Visibly nervous at the top. Grows into confidence. | The audience arc — they want to watch someone *become* brave. |
| **Overconfident** | Walks in like they'll never drink. Cracks by round 3. | The satisfying deflation. Essential for pacing. |
| **Wildcard** | Says the thing no one expected. Derails a moment in the best way. | This is a *behavior*, not a person. Both participants should be capable of Wildcard moments. |
| **Super Fun** | The infectious energy that makes the viewer wish they were at the table. | Again, a behavior. If neither participant generates this, the episode dies. |

**For JESS & MARCO specifically:** We want Jess to carry Underdog → Hero energy (nervous opener, raw honesty by mid-episode). Marco should open as Overconfident with Villain instincts (instigator, follow-up asker) but reveal Underdog vulnerability when the questions turn on him. The *swap* is the episode's emotional engine.

---

## What We Value (Ranked)

### 1) Comfort on Camera Under Pressure
Not "good on camera." Comfort *under pressure.* There's a difference. We need people whose faces do interesting things when they're caught off guard — who don't retreat into a media-trained mask. Test: ask them an uncomfortable question in the casting call with zero warm-up. Watch what their hands do.

### 2) Experience with Vulnerability Formats
Podcasts, confessional TikToks, therapy-speak fluency, even just being "the friend everyone tells everything to." We need people who have *practiced* being honest in semi-public contexts. Not performers. Practitioners. [[H02: Participants with prior vulnerability-format experience (confessional content, improv, group therapy) produce 3.1x more "save/share" moments per minute than traditional on-camera talent|vulnerability practice predicts shareability]].

### 3) Social Native Fluency
They should understand the *grammar* of internet content without being calculating about it. They know what a "moment" feels like because they've consumed thousands of them — not because they're trying to manufacture one. They use platform language naturally (not cringe-forced). They understand that a pause can be louder than a punchline.

### 4) Group Chemistry Instinct
Can they read another person's energy and adjust? Do they know when to push and when to soften? Do they laugh *with* or *at*? In a two-person format, chemistry isn't a bonus — it's the entire structural foundation. We cast the *pairing*, not the individuals.

---

## Red Flags to Avoid

| Red Flag | Why |
|---|---|
| **Follower count as primary qualifier** | Vanity metric. A 500K following built on thirst traps tells us nothing about vulnerability capacity. We've seen 2K-follower participants outperform influencers 4:1 on engagement per impression. |
| **Traditional acting credits** | Trained actors default to *performance* under pressure. We need *reaction.* If they've done improv or sketch, that's different — that rewards authenticity. But if their reel is monologues and scene work, they'll "act" their way through the episode and the audience will feel it. |
| **Brand deal history** | Anyone whose content is primarily sponsored has been trained to protect an image. Truth or Drink requires image *destruction.* They'll self-censor at the critical moment. |
| **"I'm an open book" self-description** | People who say this are almost never actually open. They've pre-packaged a version of openness. We want people who are *surprised by their own honesty.* |
| **Over-preparation** | If they ask for the questions in advance, pass. The format's power is the unscripted moment. |

---

## Gender Mix & Regional Voice

**Gender Mix:** This episode pairs a woman (Jess, she/her) and a man (Marco, he/him). Across the *series,* we need to rotate through: woman/woman, man/man, non-binary pairings, mixed groups of 3-4, and stranger pairings. No episode should feel like it's performing progressiveness — it should feel like the natural variety of who Gen Z actually is.

**Regional Voice:** This is where most casting directors fail. Gen Z is not monolithically coastal. We need:

- Midwest earnestness (the person who says something devastating with zero irony)
- Southern storytelling rhythm (the person who takes the long way to the point and makes it worth it)
- Northeast speed and directness (the person who answers before the question is finished)
- West Coast emotional vocabulary (the person who names the feeling)
- First-gen American code-switching (the person who carries two cultural frameworks and lets both show)

[[H03: Regional vocal diversity in cast produces 27% higher comment-section engagement due to increased "I know someone exactly like this" identification responses|accent as algorithm fuel]].

For THIS episode: Jess should carry a non-LA energy — Midwest, Southeast, or first-gen vocal texture. Marco should feel East Coast or Southwest — fast-talking but warm underneath.

# PART II: FULL VIDEO SCRIPT

**CUT ANALYTICS — TRUTH OR DRINK: GEN Z EDITION**

## SCENE 1: COLD OPEN HOOK (0:00–0:15)

**[STAGE DIRECTION: COLD OPEN — No music. Hard cut to a TIGHT CLOSE-UP on MARCO's face, mid-reaction. His eyes go wide. A half-laugh dies in his throat. He looks directly into the lens for one beat too long.]**

**MARCO:**
*(barely audible)*
Bro. You can't just… *ask* that.

**[SMASH CUT TO: Wide shot. The table. Two chairs. A bottle of tequila between them. Cards face-down. JESS is already reaching for the bottle.]**

**JESS:**
*(laughing, panicked)*
I'm drinking. I'm DRINKING. Don't even —

**[CUT TO BLACK. Title card: TRUTH OR DRINK. Beat. Then underneath: GEN Z EDITION.]**

---

## SCENE 2: INTRODUCTIONS (0:15–0:55)

**[STAGE DIRECTION: WIDE TWO-SHOT. Jess and Marco sit across from each other. Lighting is warm but not soft — we want texture on their faces. The set is minimal: table, chairs, bottle, shot glasses, card deck. No branded anything. A single wilting houseplant in the background because it's funny and Gen Z will notice it.]**

**JESS:**
Okay. I'm Jess, I'm twenty-two, she/her, and I'm already stressed.

**MARCO:**
I'm Marco, twenty-one, he/him, and I'm not stressed at all.

**JESS:**
*(to camera)*
He's lying. His leg is bouncing so hard the table's shaking.

**MARCO:**
*(looks down, laughs)*
That's just… how I sit.

**JESS:**
Sure.

**[STAGE DIRECTION: This is the chemistry check. If this exchange doesn't land naturally — if there's any stiffness, any "waiting for their turn to talk" energy — the episode won't work. The edit should feel like we caught the middle of a conversation, not the beginning of one.]**

**MARCO:**
So how do we know each other? Should we…?

**JESS:**
We were in the same group project freshman year and he did *nothing* —

**MARCO:**
That is — okay, that's *partially* true —

**JESS:**
— and somehow we became best friends after I yelled at him about it.

**MARCO:**
She didn't yell. She sent me a Google Doc with a "feedback" section. It was more devastating than yelling.

**JESS:**
*(grinning)*
I stand by that doc.

---

## SCENE 3: RULES & POUR (0:55–1:15)

**[STAGE DIRECTION: CLOSE-UP on the tequila being poured into two shot glasses. The sound design here matters — the liquid hitting glass should be crisp and slightly ominous.]**

**JESS (V.O.):**
Rules are simple. You pull a card. You read it to the other person. They either answer truthfully… or they drink.

**MARCO (V.O.):**
And the questions get worse.

**JESS (V.O.):**
They get *more honest.*

**MARCO (V.O.):**
…That's what I said.

---

## SCENE 4: ROUND 1 — WARM-UP (1:15–2:30)

**[STAGE DIRECTION: Medium two-shot. Energy should be playful. These questions are softballs designed to establish rhythm and let the audience settle into the dynamic.]**

**MARCO:**
*(pulls card, reads)*
"What's the most embarrassing thing on your phone's search history?"

**JESS:**
Oh, that's easy. "How to tell if your therapist actually likes you."

**MARCO:**
*(genuine laugh)*
Wait, that's so real though.

**JESS:**
I know! That's why it's embarrassing! Because it's *real.*

**[STAGE DIRECTION: This is the first "moment." Jess's answer is funny AND vulnerable. The audience should feel the door open slightly. Marco's reaction — genuine, not performed — validates her. This is the chemistry contract with the viewer: these two are safe with each other, so the danger of the questions will feel earned, not exploitative.]**

**JESS:**
*(pulls card, reads)*
"What's one thing you pretend to understand but absolutely don't?"

**MARCO:**
Crypto.

**JESS:**
Everyone says crypto.

**MARCO:**
Okay fine. *(pause)* Emotional availability.

**JESS:**
*(slow blink)*
…We're gonna come back to that.

**MARCO:**
Please don't.

**JESS:**
We're *absolutely* coming back to that.

---

## SCENE 5: ROUND 2 — ESCALATION (2:30–4:00)

**[STAGE DIRECTION: The lighting hasn't changed but the ENERGY has. Tighter shots. Longer pauses in the edit. We start holding on faces a beat longer than comfortable. The music bed — if any — drops out entirely.]**

**MARCO:**
*(pulls card, reads it silently first. His face changes.)*
Okay. *(reads aloud)* "Have you ever stayed in a friendship out of guilt? Are you doing it right now?"

**[STAGE DIRECTION: HOLD on Jess. Three full seconds of silence. This is the pivot point of the episode. The audience needs to feel the weight of the question landing.]**

**JESS:**
*(long exhale)*
Yes. And… *(looks at Marco)* not this one. But yes.

**MARCO:**
You don't have to say who.

**JESS:**
I know. *(beat)* It's my roommate. And I think she knows. And I think that's why it's gotten weird. Because we're both pretending.

**[STAGE DIRECTION: This is the Underdog → Hero turn. Jess could have drunk. She chose honesty, and not the safe kind — the kind that implicates her in something uncomfortable. The audience will feel protective of her here. Marco's job in this moment is to RECEIVE, not react. No jokes. No deflection. Just presence.]**

**MARCO:**
*(quietly)*
That's really honest.

**JESS:**
*(laughs, eyes slightly wet)*
That's literally the game, Marco.

**[BEAT.]**

**JESS:**
*(pulls card, reads)*
"What's something you've never told me that you probably should have?"

**[STAGE DIRECTION: CLOSE-UP on MARCO. This is where his Overconfident armor should visibly crack. He's been the funny one, the deflector, the instigator. Now the camera is on HIM and there's nowhere to go.]**

**MARCO:**
*(reaches for the bottle)*

**JESS:**
Marco.

**MARCO:**
*(hand on bottle, doesn't pour)*

**JESS:**
You don't have to.

**MARCO:**
*(pulls hand back)*
When you and Danny broke up last year, I… didn't check in on you. And it wasn't because I forgot. It was because I didn't know how. And I've felt weird about it since.

**JESS:**
*(pause)*
I noticed.

**MARCO:**
I know.

**JESS:**
I wasn't mad. I was just… sad. That you couldn't.

**MARCO:**
I'm sorry.

**JESS:**
I know. *(small smile)* That's why we're still friends.

**[STAGE DIRECTION: THIS is the episode's emotional core. Two people negotiating a real, unresolved thing between them — on camera, unrehearsed. The audience should feel like they're witnessing something private. The edit should resist the urge to cut away. Stay on their faces. Let the silence breathe. This is the moment that gets screen-recorded and posted with the caption "I wasn't ready for this."]**

## SCENE 6: ROUND 3 — RELEASE VALVE (4:00–5:15)

**[STAGE DIRECTION: The energy needs to shift. After the emotional peak, we need a RELEASE — not a retreat. The questions should be lighter but still sharp. The laughter here should feel earned, like the relief after a hard conversation.]**

**MARCO:**
*(pulls card, already smiling)*
Oh thank God. *(reads)* "What's your most unhinged 3 AM thought?"

**JESS:**
That I'm going to end up exactly like my mom but with worse skin.

**MARCO:**
*(choking)*
JESS.

**JESS:**
What! She's great! She just has a lot of opinions about my life choices and I can feel myself becoming that!

**MARCO:**
Your skin is fine.

**JESS:**
THAT'S what you took from that?

**[They're both laughing. The tension from Scene 5 is metabolized, not erased. This is important — Gen Z audiences can tell when a show "resets" artificially. The laughter here works BECAUSE of what came before it.]**

**JESS:**
*(pulls card)*
"Who in this room would you trust with your unlocked phone?"

**MARCO:**
*(immediately)*
Not you.

**JESS:**
WHAT?

**MARCO:**
You would read every text and then bring it up in casual conversation three months later with ZERO warning —

**JESS:**
I would NEVER — okay I would absolutely do that.

**MARCO:**
SEE.

**JESS:**
But like, lovingly!

**MARCO:**
There's no loving version of that!

## SCENE 7: FINAL QUESTION (5:15–6:15)

**[STAGE DIRECTION: WIDE SHOT. Both of them have had a couple of shots by now. Not drunk — loosened. The final card is face-down between them. Neither reaches for it immediately.]**

**MARCO:**
Last one?

**JESS:**
Last one.

**[MARCO picks up the card. Reads it. Slides it across the table to JESS.]**

**JESS:**
*(reads aloud)*
"After today, will anything between you two actually change?"

**[STAGE DIRECTION: HOLD. Let them sit with it. This is the meta-question — the one that breaks the fourth wall of the format. It asks: was this real, or was this content?]**

**JESS:**
*(to Marco, not to camera)*
I think I'm gonna text my roommate tonight.

**MARCO:**
*(nods)*
I think I'm gonna be better at checking in.

**JESS:**
With me?

**MARCO:**
With everyone. *(beat)* Starting with you.

**JESS:**
*(raises shot glass)*
To being bad at feelings but trying anyway.

**MARCO:**
*(raises glass)*
The Gen Z motto.

**[They clink. They drink. Not because they're avoiding the truth — because they answered it and the drink is a celebration, not an escape.]**

## POST-SCRIPT: PRODUCTION NOTES FOR CASTING

**What makes this script work is what ISN'T on the page.** The pauses. The almost-reaching-for-the-bottle. The eye contact that says more than the dialogue. You cannot script those moments — you can only **cast for them.**

When evaluating talent for Jess and Marco, run this test:

1. **The Silence Test:** Ask them a personal question and then say nothing for 10 seconds. Do they fill the silence with nervous chatter, or do they let it sit? We want people who can *hold* a moment.

2. **The Pivot Test:** Tell them something funny, then immediately ask something serious. How fast can they shift registers? Gen Z communication is *tonal whiplash* — funny-sad-funny-deep in the span of 30 seconds.

3. **The Generosity Test:** Put two finalists together and watch who asks follow-up questions. The person who is curious about the OTHER person — not performing their own story — is the one we want.

**Final note:** This episode lives or dies on the moment in Scene 5 where Marco almost reaches for the bottle and then doesn't. That's not a scripted beat. That's a *casting* beat. Find the person who will instinctively reach — and then choose honesty instead.

That's the show.
