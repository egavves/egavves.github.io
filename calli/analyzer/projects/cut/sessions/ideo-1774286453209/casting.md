## PART I: CASTING BLUEPRINT

---

### Demographics & Energy

**Age Range:** 19–26 (hard boundaries). Sweet spot is 21–24 — old enough to drink on camera, young enough that the questions still sting.

**Personality Types We Want in the Room:**
- The Overthinker who narrates their own spiral
- The Chaotic Empath who will cry *and* make everyone else laugh about it
- The Deadpan Realist who delivers devastating honesty like a weather report
- The Nervous Talker who fills silence with gold
- At least one person who goes *quiet* before they answer — that silence is content

**Diversity (non-negotiable across every axis):**
- Race, ethnicity, cultural background — minimum 4 distinct lived experiences at the table
- Gender spectrum representation including non-binary and trans participants
- Body diversity — no "influencer body type" monoculture
- Neurodivergent perspectives welcomed and accommodated in format design
- Socioeconomic range — not everyone should be from a coastal city or a college campus
- At least one participant whose first language isn't English (bilingual moments are viral moments)

**Energy Calibration:** We're casting a *room*, not individuals. The table should feel like a group chat that somehow met in person. We need tension, tenderness, and at least one person who makes everyone else braver.

---

### Character Archetypes Needed

| Archetype | What This Looks Like | Why We Need Them |
|---|---|---|
| **THE HERO** | Not the loudest — the most *honest*. Goes first on the hardest question. Doesn't perform vulnerability, just... is vulnerable. Others look at them for permission to be real. | Anchors emotional credibility. Without them, it's a party. With them, it's a moment. |
| **THE VILLAIN** | Not actually villainous — just *unbothered*. Says the thing everyone's thinking. Refuses to soften their answer. Might choose "drink" on a question everyone expects them to answer, just to watch the room react. | Creates productive friction. [[H01:casting_friction_test|Pairing a "Villain" archetype with a "Hero" generates 3.2x more comment-section debate than homogeneous energy tables.]] |
| **THE UNDERDOG** | Visibly nervous at the top. Might fidget, might laugh too hard. But by Round 3, they're the one who drops the most devastating answer. The audience *roots* for this person. | Narrative arc in a 12-minute video. Their transformation IS the story. |
| **THE OVERCONFIDENT** | Walks in like they've done this before (they haven't). Big energy. Big claims. Then a question hits them sideways and we watch the mask slip — that's the clip. | Contrast engine. Their confidence makes their crack moment 10x more compelling. |
| **THE WILDCARD** | Unpredictable in a way that's *generative*, not destructive. Might answer a question with a question. Might tell a story that goes somewhere no one expected. You genuinely don't know what they'll do. | Keeps the edit alive. Every round needs at least one moment the producers didn't see coming. |
| **THE SUPER FUN** | Pure serotonin. Not shallow — just genuinely delighted to be here. Laughs in a way that's contagious. Turns tense moments into communal moments. The room breathes because they're in it. | Emotional release valve. Without this archetype, vulnerability formats tip into therapy sessions. [[H02:levity_retention_test|Episodes with a dedicated "levity anchor" show 22% higher watch-through past the 7-minute mark.]] |

**Critical Note:** These are *energies*, not boxes. One person can carry two archetypes. The Underdog can also be the Wildcard. The Super Fun can also be the Hero. Cast for range, not rigidity.

---

### What We Value (Ranked)

**1. Comfort on Camera Under Pressure**
Not "good on camera." *Comfortable when it gets uncomfortable.* We test this in casting by asking one real question with the camera already rolling before they know they're being evaluated. Do they freeze, perform, or just... be a person? We want the third thing.

**2. Experience with Vulnerability Formats**
Have they ever been in a room — on camera or off — where the expectation was radical honesty? Podcast guests, support group facilitators, people who've done storytelling nights, people who've gone viral for an unfiltered moment. We're not looking for "brave" as a personality trait. We're looking for people who've *practiced* being honest in front of others.

**3. Social Native Fluency**
They understand the grammar of short-form content *instinctively*. They know what a "clip moment" feels like from the inside. They don't need to be creators — they need to be literate. When a moment is happening, they don't break it by looking at the camera or mugging. They stay in it because they understand the format is watching.

**4. Group Chemistry Instinct**
Can they read a room? Do they know when to talk and when to let someone else's moment breathe? Do they build on what someone else said or just wait for their turn? We test this in group casting rounds — the person who *listens* is almost always more valuable than the person who *talks*.

---

### Red Flags to Avoid

| Red Flag | Why It Kills the Video |
|---|---|
| **Follower count as a qualifier** | Vanity metric. A person with 800 followers who makes their friends cry-laugh at dinner is worth more than someone with 200K who's optimized for brand safety. Follower count selects for *polish*, and polish is the enemy of this format. |
| **Traditional acting credits** | Trained actors default to *performing* emotion rather than *having* it. The audience clocks this in under 3 seconds. If someone's instinct when a hard question lands is to "play" surprised rather than *be* surprised, the whole table's credibility collapses. |
| **Brand deal history as a positive signal** | Brand-friendly creators have spent years learning what *not* to say. We need people who haven't been trained to protect a parasocial relationship with an audience. We need people who'll say the thing that might lose them followers. |
| **"I'm an open book" self-description** | People who say this are almost never actually open. They've just pre-approved a set of "vulnerable" stories they're comfortable performing. Real openness looks like hesitation, not confidence. |
| **Over-preparation / asking for questions in advance** | The format dies if answers are rehearsed. We share the *theme* (e.g., "questions about your family"), never the specific questions. Anyone who pushes back on this is protecting a version of themselves we don't need. |

---

### Gender Mix & Regional Voice

**Gender Mix:**
- Target: No more than 50% of any single gender identity at the table
- For a 5-person round: aim for 2-2-1 or 2-1-1-1 configurations across the spectrum
- Avoid all-male or all-female tables *unless* that's the specific episode concept — and even then, ensure diversity within that group
- Non-binary and gender-nonconforming participants should be present as *themselves*, not as "the non-binary one." If the edit reduces someone to their identity label, we've failed.

**Regional Voice:**
- At minimum, 2 of 5 participants should be from outside the LA/NYC content ecosystem
- Actively recruit from: the South, the Midwest, rural communities, military towns, border towns, tribal lands, immigrant enclaves in mid-size cities
- Regional voice isn't just accent — it's *reference frame*. Someone from rural Ohio and someone from East Oakland will answer "What's the biggest lie you've told your parents?" from completely different universes. That gap is where the content lives.
- [[H03:regional_diversity_engagement|Tables with 3+ distinct regional backgrounds generate 40% more "I feel so seen" comments, the highest-signal engagement metric for Gen Z audiences.]]

### INTRODUCTIONS (0:15–1:30)

**[STAGE DIRECTION:** Camera is already rolling when participants sit down. We catch the awkward "where do I sit" energy. No one is told to introduce themselves — the PRODUCER prompts from off-camera. Handheld. Intimate. The table is small enough that knees touch.**]**

**PRODUCER** *(off-camera):*
"Alright. Just your name, your age, and... one thing you've never said on camera before."

**[Beat. Everyone looks at each other. Nervous laughter.]**

**PARTICIPANT A** *(The Underdog — 21, fidgeting with a ring, won't make eye contact with the lens):*
"Um. I'm [Name]. I'm twenty-one. And... okay, I've never said on camera that I... I still sleep with the light on. Like, fully on. Lamp. Overhead. All of it."

**PARTICIPANT B** *(The Overconfident — 24, leans back, arms wide, owns the chair):*
"That's actually adorable? I'm [Name], twenty-four, and something I've never said on camera... I think I'm the funniest person in most rooms I'm in. Like, genuinely."

**PARTICIPANT D** *(The Super Fun — 22, already grinning):*
"The AUDACITY—"

**PARTICIPANT C** *(The Hero — 23, calm, direct eye contact with the camera):*
"I'm [Name]. Twenty-three. And I've never said on camera that I went to therapy for the first time six weeks ago. So. Yeah. Here we are."

**[The room shifts. Participant A looks at C with visible respect. Participant B uncrosses their arms — first micro-crack.]**

**PARTICIPANT E** *(The Wildcard — 20, sitting cross-legged in the chair, already poured themselves a shot "just to have it ready"):*
"I'm [Name], I'm twenty, and I've never said on camera that I think most people are performing being alive and I find it exhausting. Can we start?"

**PARTICIPANT D:**
"I love you already."
